### The Origin of the Olympic Games and the Revival of Modern World Games

[Special Feature by Our Newspaper] The origin and cause of the Olympic Games have long been lost to history. Judging from the fact that Iphitos in 776 BC was called the "reviver" of the Olympics, the founding of the Olympics dates back more than 2,700 years.

The Olympic Games, since Iphitos restarted it, lasted for thirteen centuries. In 394 AD, the host, Roman King Theodosius, considering the declining morality of the competitors, who often resorted to various despicable means to win the championship, believed that the original spirit of promoting sports and connecting emotions had been completely lost. Therefore, he ordered a permanent ban on the holding of the Olympics. Soon, the statue of Zeus, the king of the Greek gods, was also burned, and the Olympics was suspended for more than 1,500 years.

Revival of the Modern Olympic Games

The revival of the modern Olympic Games, becoming a global event held every four years, must be attributed to Sir Coubertin, a French soldier and scholar. From the army, the knight observed that a sound physique could only come from sports training. Furthermore, from the history of Greece, he studied that the decline of ancient countries was also greatly related to the Olympics. Thus, he engaged in launching a grand World Games plan, hoping that the World Games could connect the cultures of various countries, eliminate the barriers between various ethnic groups, thereby eliminating world war and achieving lasting peace.

In 1893, the knight launched the first step of the plan, sending letters in his private capacity to sports leaders of various countries, suggesting a meeting in Paris the following year to discuss methods for the resumption of the Olympics. As a result of the meeting, it was unanimously passed to hold the first modern World Games in 1896, and it was stipulated that it would be held every four years thereafter.

The First Session Held in Athens

The first World Games were held in Athens, the capital of Greece, to commemorate the ancient Olympics. Thanks to the donation of a nobleman, a large marble stadium, which was already hailed as magnificent and majestic at the time, was built in this ancient cultural capital.

After a series of arduous preparations, the modern Olympics finally opened. Everything was just beginning, and only twelve units participated in the competition. The events were also simple and basic, but they had already aroused the attention of enlightened people around the world, opening up a broad path for the subsequent World Games.

After that, the second, third, fourth, and fifth sessions were held every four years in Paris, St. Louis, London, and Stockholm, respectively. Although the third session had only eight units participating due to the long distance and the ongoing Russo-Japanese War, with a turbulent situation, the World Games were indeed progressing towards perfection, one session better than the previous.

European War Destroys the World Games

Sir Coubertin's ideal was shattered. In 1916, the Olympics not only failed to eliminate war, but was burned down by war. The sixth session was thus aborted.

In 1920, the seventh session was held in the capital of Belgium, which suffered the most from the ravages of the god of war during the Great War. Twenty-five units participated, with 117 events, breaking the records of the previous sessions.

In 1924, Paris hosted the Games for the second time, with forty-five countries participating, and the events gradually became more complete.

The ninth World Games were held in Amsterdam, the capital of the Netherlands. Four thousand athletes from forty-three countries participated in the competition. The ninth session made a major and successful attempt, establishing women's track and field events for the first time.

Our Country's Historic Attempt

In 1932, in the midst of the peak of the world economic crisis, the tenth session was held in Los Angeles. Although due to economic reasons, the number of athletes sent by various countries was far smaller than in previous sessions, the quality was far superior to any previous session. Sixteen world records and twenty-five conference records were broken in this gathering of heroes. Our country also made a historic attempt, sending Liu Changchun, an athlete, for the first time. Although he failed due to natural and acquired reasons, it also renewed the eyes of foreigners and corrected many absurd imaginations of foreigners about China in terms of international perception.

After the first World War, Germany, which became increasingly powerful, hosted the eleventh session in 1936. The scientific mind and fighting spirit of the Germans made this session unprecedented. The largest sports stadium and the most well-equipped World Games Village impressed athletes from fifty-one countries and thousands of tourists from all over the world. It is regrettable that this global conference has reached its peak in terms of material resources, but the spiritual gains are pitifully scarce. The mad war drove away the god of peace again, and the twelfth and thirteenth sessions were aborted in the womb.

The first session after the war has been scheduled to open in London today. In fact, this session is only the twelfth, but from 1896, it has been the fourteenth "Olympiad" (Olmpiad in Greek, originally meaning a four-year period). (Xin)

### Snippets of New Conversations (In Nanjing)

 Bai Yong

The electricity is cut off one day out of every three. Tonight, it is again a world of darkness. Dark clouds cover the sky, and even the expected moon offers no light. The indoor temperature is a hundred degrees. Everyone is cooling off in the courtyard. Although there isn't much of a breeze to be found, at least the air is stirring a bit. Unfortunately, the rain is coming again, so we have no choice but to trim the candle by the west window. The price of each "Monk's Cap" brand candle has reached 500,000, but there's no other option.

Coincidentally, guests arrived, perfectly fitting the ancient saying of trimming candles and talking in the night rain. The two guests could be called "old friends" or "new friends." As for the former, we were all poor fellow villagers to begin with, and our conversations have something in common. As for the latter, they have already leaped from being poor to becoming "bosses," which puts them in a different category from me, this white-haired scholar who will grow old under the window. However, listening to their "wild tales" is still a source of historical material, "therefore, I record them."

Guest A: "Today, pork is 900,000, and a 'jin' (catty) is only fifteen taels. But it's not fixed. At Shuixi Gate, it sells for 800,000, at Caixia Street it's 640,000, and at Xinqiao it's 720,000. Look, what counts as the standard! The price of soybean sprouts is completely different: 120,000, 100,000, and even 150,000!"

Me: "Let's not talk about this. I heard you've been making a lot of money recently. What business are you doing exactly?"

Guest B: "Don't you know? He's been trafficking in livestock. The last time it was more than 3 billion!"

Me: "Oh? How does that work?"

B (smiling at A): "There was a major flood in Jiangbei this year, and the situation in the Suxian area is even worse. Added to that is the fighting. Livestock are all being sold at low prices. A horse costs no more than 50 million. You can easily earn over 100 million in Nanjing. The villagers are easy to deceive. They don't necessarily pay in cash. After the purchase is settled, they pay a hundred or two upfront, and the rest is paid with promissory notes. Selling on credit for ten days or half a month is nothing. With a quick turnaround, you can make money hand over fist."

A (uneasy, but also self-satisfied): "Don't listen to his nonsense. He stockpiled gasoline half a month ago, and now he's already doubled his profit. Today, he asked me to buy used oil drums. Last week, a drum was 400,000, but in the last few days, it's risen to 220,000, even those made from tin can scraps. Do you know why? Public school employees are allocated oil, and everyone has to buy containers to collect it, so there's been a big fluctuation in the market. It's true, even broken tiles have their day!"

Me: "I never thought that you, who used to be in the used book business and looked down on gold and silver, would be getting involved in this now?"

B: "Can books still be sold? A ream of newsprint costs over 5,000, and a single sheet sells for up to 200,000. Any old book costs over a million. Even if people have money, who would buy these things? Used books are even worse. Firstly, no one reads them, and secondly, the prices don't go up. Look, those used bookstores in Zhuangyuanjing are all selling by weight. The shelves are full of Feng Yuqi novels and patriotic songs. We, who talk about Song and Yuan editions, don't even bother! Several new bookstores from Beiping came to Jiangnan hoping to strike it rich, but they were all disappointed. Those who are willing to use their brains don't sell new books and instead switch back to stationery. Look, isn't a Parker fountain pen equivalent to US dollars? What we're doing is nothing!"

Generally, when Western men encounter women on the street, they naturally remove their hats as a sign of respect. In fact, this custom of removing hats to women evolved from an ancient law, the main purpose of which was to force "all inferiors to remove their head coverings before superiors as a sign of respect."

This law can be traced back even earlier to the custom of stripping prisoners of war of their clothing. In ancient times, the Assyrians stripped all prisoners of war of their clothes, showing their complete subjugation. Therefore, nudity was regarded as a form of worship. Even now, the custom of nudity to show respect for "superiors" still exists in some primitive tribal peoples.

In any case, there is no doubt that the warriors unknowingly passed on the habit of "inferiors" respecting "superiors" to later generations.

When the samurai system declined, men changed to wearing hats, and they retained the samurai custom of removing helmets for women.

### Random Thoughts on Jing'an Temple

 Chen Zuogao

Jing'an Temple was a famous place for escaping the heat and spending the summer in the late Qing Dynasty. Every summer, tourists came in droves. As the sun set, groups of people would arrive with fans and light shirts. Some brought their families, some brought their concubines. Decorated carriages turned and jade wheels rolled. Even late at night, among the sounds of pines and bamboos, there were still hairpins and earrings left behind. Today, there is an ancient well in front of the temple, located next to a streetcar station, called the Sixth Spring Under Heaven. During the Guangxu period of the Qing Dynasty, a tea house was built on top of it. Tang Jingsong, the famous minister of the Sino-French War, once went to the temple with the Chaozhou scholar Xiao Daonong to see the ancient well. The spring water seemed to be boiling, which was the case every year. They went upstairs for tea cakes. "Women surrounded the seats, charming and lovely, but could not endure the cold and returned." See Tang's "Please Enlist" Diary, entry for October 23rd of the Guangxu 12th year.

Shen Garden was located next to the temple. Huang Xietong's "Record of Dreams in Songnan" described the place as having painted beams and pearl curtains, rising and falling with the morning and evening, showing the grandeur of the buildings and the elegance of the furnishings. When tired of walking, one could lean on the carved railing and sip bitter tea. A fresh breeze would blow away all worldly worries. Sometimes, flower branches would reach out from across the seat, beckoning to people. The sound of hairpins and the shadows of ornaments seemed to be in the curtain, and one would often recite Yuan Xiang's "Drunk, I hear the flowers rising; waking, I hear the orioles," lingering and unable to leave. Downstairs, there were several billiard tables where one could gamble under the moonlight. Western food was also available, but guests had to order in advance to avoid being caught unprepared. It is said that while drinking tea, there were also singers performing to add to the fun. A Shanghai ballad said, "With an appointment at Shen Garden to enjoy tea, hastily ascending the seat to play the pipa. After the song ends, she goes down to the singing building, and the horse-drawn carriages linger at the door." This probably refers to this.

Thirty-two years ago, when I was free on holidays, I would come with old scholars from Zhejiang to the temple to chat over tea. Forerunners such as Gao Yehou and Ding Fuzhi also attended. I occasionally sat at the end of the table, enjoying the eloquent conversations. Thinking back to the temple in those days, flowers were dense, brocade layers, forever soft dust fragrant, and various other beauties filled it. But now the temple grounds are narrower, the scenery is completely different, the surrounding buildings are crowded together, and vehicles converge, just like what Bao Mingyuan called "four-way intersections." Alas, in this noisy city, who would still see it as a scenic spot! Reflecting on the past and thinking about the present, how can one not feel a sense of vicissitudes?

### The Problem of Maids

 Qifeng

Before the war, hiring one or two maids was not a big deal, but now, in this era of high prices, the problem of maids is quite serious.

As for maids, according to what I've learned from the housewives among my relatives and friends, most of them are complaining constantly. They feel that they can't do without them, but they get angry when they hire them. This is because maids are no longer as "loyal and obedient" as they used to be. The times have instilled in them new knowledge. They have a clear and quick understanding of the decline in currency value and the soaring prices. They are no longer a group of uneducated villagers who can be bullied.

Although maids have not yet progressed to the point of organizing trade unions, their ability to transmit information is no less effective than a perfect organization. In an alley, the amount of wages at a certain house and how the wages are calculated are all found out clearly. If they have fellow villagers or girlfriends in other areas, they also meet frequently to discuss the issue of wages. If you pay them less, they will compare your wages with those of other houses and ask you to increase them. Otherwise, they will find a very good lie, and with the introduction of fellow villagers or friends, they will jump to another house. If they are not satisfied, they will jump again. So often, when your maid leaves saying she is returning to her hometown, after a considerable period of time, she will be discovered at your relatives' or friends' house, which is truly a feeling of laughter and tears.

As for wages, it seems that those who are paid a fixed wage are the most backward and honest. Although the housewife will increase the wage, it is never enough. The second type is based on rice as the standard, ranging from three to four "dou" (pecks) per month. They hope that the price of rice will rise every day. The third type is based on the cost of living index, with monthly "base salaries" of six or seven yuan. ### Fruit Names

 Gongsun Wu

"Bolo" (pineapple) is not "boluomi" (jackfruit). The "Dictionary of Sources" explains it very clearly. Perhaps the owners of fruit stalls are unaware of this. Although our country is large, it is now unified, and Taiwan has also been recovered. And freight transportation is now more convenient than in the past. Family treasures and named items should not be confused because of geographical distance. It is especially inappropriate to gain the upper hand by creating differences. Fine things may have different names, but they are not convenient to say. Fruits from California or Australia are not renamed when they arrive in New York or London. If there is confusion in names, it is no different from calling a deer a horse. Take sunflower leaf fans, for example. In the Shanghai market, they are called banana leaf fans. Sunflower leaf fans mostly come from Xinhui, but Jiangsu and Guangdong are not far apart. Why must their names be changed? There are several banana trees in the Shanghai Public Concession. Banana trees are often seen in paintings. The ancients even wrote on banana leaves. How can this be the same thing as sunflower leaves that have been flattened, dried, and made into fans? Wang Yisun was from Kuaiji. His poems contain the line "sunflower fans are hard to keep." The sunflower was known as a sunflower by the Song people. Grapes are planted in Guangdong. They ripen early and are sour. The Cantonese mistakenly call them "bodhi seeds," which is quite ridiculous. It should be pronounced "jia" in "fanru" (foreign eggplant), but the people of Shanghai like to pronounce it "jia," and "fan" is gradually becoming more cautious. In the north, this thing is sometimes called "fire persimmon." A common thing has multiple names, which proves that the unification of language still has a way to go. The "wen dan" (pomelo) and "zhulei" (another kind of pomelo) are slightly different, but there is no need to force a division. The word "pomelo" is enough. The matter of amnesty has been rumored for a long time, and there are many different opinions. Some explain it from the perspective of authority (such as the question of whether the Legislative Yuan should propose it, which the Judicial Yuan thinks is possible), some speak from the perspective of facts (such as the opinion of Court President Xia Jun that the amnesty should not be too large), some dislike the remnants of feudalism, and some even focus on the "traitor" aspect. However, examining historical precedents, it is not purely a matter of the ruler's grace, nor is it just a question of reducing or waiving the punishment of criminals. The true purpose of "pardon" and "general amnesty" is that "everyone participates in renewal," which has the highest political and benevolent significance.

Originally, the crimes of criminals are mostly caused by objective conditions. As Western civilization advances, punishments are gradually reduced, and there are also "rehabilitation" and "probation," doing everything possible to reveal the original circumstances and teach people to move upward, all in line with moral philosophy and political principles. The improvement of criminals is the common progress of the ruling class and the intellectual class. This is where civilized countries differ from barbarous ones. Chinese sages often praised "weeping for crime after dismounting the carriage" and "feeling pity rather than joy when understanding the circumstances," all of which have the meaning of forgiveness and mutual blame. The ordinary causes of crime, such as "economic oppression" (the so-called "cold and hunger lead to theft"), are the responsibility of misgovernment; if knowledge is low (greed and violence are all due to insufficient education), then the responsibility lies in mis-education. Therefore, at each autumn review ceremony, ancient emperors reduced their meals and wore plain clothes to express their regret.

As for amnesty, it is only one kind of "grace." "General amnesty" is not limited to imperial celebrations, and the "imperial grace" of imperial celebrations is not limited to "general amnesty." Therefore, "general amnesty" and "imperial grace" cannot be discussed together. The most famous amnesty in history was during the Tang Dynasty's Xinyuan Restoration, which was promulgated by Lu Xuan Gong. Even military men and brave soldiers were moved to tears, because the wording was sincere and fair, showing the unity of the ruler and the ruled. In addition to Zhu Ci, even if Zhu Tao demonstrated obedience, they were also given renewal and forgiveness. And the taxes imposed on bridge frames, bamboo and wood, and the iron monopoly were all stopped, which was a real benefit to the people. Therefore, as soon as the edict of amnesty was issued, all parties were delighted, and they turned toward the dynasty, turning danger into safety. In addition, the amnesty examples recorded in historical books, such as when celestial changes warned of danger, water and drought disasters occurred, and extraordinary disasters and chaos arose, emperors would often issue edicts to review, allowing officials to report on the abuses of both the ruler and the ruled, or report that punishments were too numerous and lawsuits were unjust, offending the harmony of heaven, and then they would consider reducing sentences, and as far as possible releasing those who were guilty of minor crimes or were of doubtful guilt. This was also the meaning of a general amnesty. Since it is said that "everyone participates in renewal," then the ruling class has a past responsibility, and not only do those who are pardoned have the opportunity to renew themselves, but those who grant the pardon must also renew themselves!

The general amnesty of imperial celebrations is not limited to the enthronement of a new emperor. For example, in the third month of Bingzi in the Song Dynasty, Emperor Taizu visited Xijing to worship heaven and earth. The old people said to each other, "Our country has rarely experienced turmoil, and today we see peace again," and some shed tears. After the sacrifice, a general amnesty was issued to express the shared celebration. At this time, it had been more than ten years since Gengshen Emperor Taizu ascended the throne. The following year, Emperor Taizong ascended the throne and changed the era name to "Taiping Xingguo" (Great Peace and Prosperity), and another general amnesty was issued, also to share the meaning of peace.

In modern times, when the former Qing emperors ascended the throne, "granting grace" was the primary meaning. "Granting" also has the meaning of being vast and universal, which is roughly the same as the "general" in "general amnesty." The first requirement of granting grace was "giving preferential treatment in the imperial examination to celebrate the list," which every emperor had to do. And the birthdays of the Empress Dowager and the Emperor were also headed by "granting preferential treatment in the imperial examination." In addition, officials were promoted and given titles, public crimes were forgiven, dismissed officials were given official titles when they followed the ranks to offer congratulations, and old people and farmers were given hat ornaments and red flowers by local officials. In places with arrears of taxes and disasters, the land tax was reduced or exempted. In short, there was always a little grace bestowed, and the general amnesty was not very noticed. Since the Republic of China, some presidents have imitated ancient emperors in issuing general amnesties upon taking office. In the 14th year, Duan Qirui, as the provisional chief executive, also issued a general amnesty decree. Following precedents, in addition to the ten unforgivable crimes, ordinary criminals were reduced or exempted, and Cao Kun was specifically mentioned as not being included in the amnesty. At that time, Mr. Shao, the editor of the Jingbao newspaper, wrote an editorial criticizing this. Cao Kun, no matter what, could not be equated with serious criminals who committed arson and flooded rivers, and Cao was trapped in the Xinhua Palace, not having been tried, nor was he accused of any crime, then whether or not to pardon was fundamentally out of the question. Therefore, the most ridiculous general amnesty of the Republic of China was that of "Chief Executive Duan."
