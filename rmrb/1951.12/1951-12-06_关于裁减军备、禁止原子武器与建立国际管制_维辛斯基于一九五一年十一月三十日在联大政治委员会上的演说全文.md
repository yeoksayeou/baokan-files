TASS, Paris, December 1st dispatch: Mr. Chairman, distinguished representatives!

The time has come for us to summarize certain results, draw conclusions, and plan how to solve the problems before us in the future.

In this situation, it is particularly important to pick out the main, decisive issues from the issues discussed in the general debate. It is important to make new efforts to explain in detail and clearly each person's attitude towards the issues that have caused disputes and differences, and to use every opportunity to eliminate obstacles that hinder mutual understanding of this attitude. Since during the debate, during the so-called most intense verbal battles, a considerable number of various extra branches have emerged, it is often difficult to find the correct answer to the question without removing these branches. This is especially necessary. That is to say, when summarizing the results and engaging in the precise explanation of certain clauses, it is impossible to do it without removing these extra branches, without abandoning everything that has no direct relationship with these important issues but can only hinder the reaching of a unanimous decision, everything that is not conducive to calm analysis and drawing correct conclusions. This also means that it is necessary to clear up all kinds of distortions and restore the facts to their true colors and true meaning, only in this way can we ensure a correct understanding of the facts.

However, it should be pointed out that the drafters of the "Three-Power" proposal and some of the people who support this proposal have repeatedly ignored the above requirements, despite their guarantees that they are so-called striving to obtain a unanimous decision and are considering the issues entirely in accordance with the true situation and the interests of the issues themselves. This guarantee and the speeches of some representatives are absolutely not consistent. These representatives actually used various lies and clichés to attack us in their speeches, which have long been disgusting, and contain a lot of malicious and slanderous ingredients. Unfortunately, this kind of practice is repeated at every meeting, often using the same tricks, citing the same so-called facts, and quoting the same quotes, despite the fact that this attack has been defeated before, the tricks have been exposed, the lies have been refuted, and the ignorance of those who use these tricks has been exposed and revealed their ugly faces. Mock took the bankrupt old road of Austin by using the most despicable means of forgery.

Whether you like it or not, you have to deal with the attacks of these representatives again. This time, I also have to talk about the speech of Mr. Mock, the former French Minister of Interior, who said a lot of absurd and slanderous nonsense in his speech on November 26 about the so-called "Russia" occupying and "annexing" certain territories before the war and at the beginning of the war.

Mr. Mock said in his speech: Repeatedly repeating deliberate arbitrary remarks has aroused suspicion, and this suspicion held by him and those with similar psychology has been further deepened by their familiarity with certain methods of the Soviet regime. He slandered our methods, saying that the essence of these methods is to cover up the truth and other serious evils.

In order to find a basis for this slanderous fabrication, Mock even quoted words from our great teacher Lenin's work "Left-Wing Communism: An Infantile Disorder", of course, he tampered with these words, took them out of context, and therefore used obviously fraudulent methods.

But Mr. Mock became a victim of forgery. Three years ago, the American representative Austin arbitrarily forged in this place in Paris. I can't help but recall that Mr. Austin also rashly quoted – distorting and deleting the original text and true meaning of the quote – "Left-Wing Communism: An Infantile Disorder" based on what the State Department officials copied for him. These officials of the State Department neither studied nor, of course, were willing to sincerely study the essence of this issue.

Three years ago, this despicable forgery by Mr. Austin was exposed on the spot. The quotation was restored to its original form on the spot, and it proved that the tactics recommended by "Left-Wing Communism: An Infantile Disorder" to the advanced representatives of various workers' parties took into account the situation, that is, the fact that the opportunistic "leaders" would use various tricks of bourgeois diplomacy in opposing the workers' movement, and rely on the help of bourgeois governments, priests, police and courts to…insult, slander and persecute the advanced representatives of the working class. In this place, "Left-Wing Communism: An Infantile Disorder" pointed out that "we should not be afraid of difficulties, nor should we be afraid of the disruption, framing, insults and attacks by those 'leaders' (most of whom are directly or indirectly colluding with the bourgeoisie and the police because they are opportunists and social chauvinists)" (Mr. Mock, note – colluding with the police).

This is what the great and victorious banner of the working class and the entire laboring mankind, the great doctrine, Marxism-Leninism, has instructed.

It should be reminded that the method of forging quotations was used by the likes of Austin and McNair in 1948. One of their purposes was to divert the attention of world public opinion from their forgeries, especially the forgery called "M Protocol" – fabricating this protocol in order to create an excuse for the American and British occupying authorities in West Germany to suppress democratic groups in West Germany, and even prohibit people's congresses, increase pressure on trade unions, etc. Three years ago, Austin spread this kind of forgery because he sympathized with the embarrassing distress of the West German police and the French police. This year, Mr. Mock, the former French Minister of Interior, used the same trick, obviously out of the same intention and motive, which is to support the police measures taken against the advanced representatives of the working class in his own country. But for Mr. Mock, this is probably a difficult task, because in general, he obviously prefers to quote transcribed quotations, and he doesn't care at all about the fact that this quotation is distorted and forged.

Of course, this method of dealing with facts and documents cannot make the discussion fruitful in any way. Mr. Mock did not say anything else except that the three countries, the United States, Britain, and France, could only implement disarmament when they obtained sincere guarantees (the word he used was "disarmament," which is fundamentally inconsistent with the content of the "Three-Power" proposal). **Jessup's Sweet Talk and Dulles' War Cries are a Stark Contrast**

Although Mr. Mock spoke on behalf of the three countries—the United States, Britain, and France—the representatives of the United States and Britain spoke after him, both also speaking on behalf of the three countries.

In his speech, the American representative, Mr. Jessup, repeatedly tried to provide assurances that the United States was working to ease international tensions and was, in his words, advancing along the peaceful road of negotiations.

This is not the first time we have heard statements from the American, British, and French delegations expressing their willingness to reach an agreement with the Soviet Union and guarantees expressing hope to reduce the political temperature. We have already pointed out the strange division of labor in the Atlantic bloc camp. On the one hand, representatives of this bloc at the General Assembly talk glibly about peace and cooperation, about the need to ease international tensions, and express disappointment at the so-called unwillingness of the Soviet Union to compromise. But, on the other hand, equally responsible, and sometimes even more responsible, representatives of the countries of this bloc are delivering inflammatory warmongering speeches, and even directly calling for attacks on the Soviet Union and the People's Democracies. No one here dares to deny these facts. These facts are already before the world. Let whoever dares to deny these facts, deny them!

On the one hand, at the meetings of this First Committee, Mr. Jessup delivers pious speeches about peace and cooperation. On the other hand, another American representative, State Department consultant and one of the main decision-makers of American foreign policy, the famous Mr. Dulles, delivered a speech of a completely opposite nature at the annual dinner of the Detroit Advertising Association. The "New York Herald Tribune" reported that in this speech, Dulles called for the establishment of strong offensive forces distributed in convenient locations around the Soviet Union. According to Dulles, this is more necessary for every country allegedly threatened by the Soviet Union than defensive forces. Dulles declared in this speech that next year the United States will spend 60 billion dollars on expanding its military. He also said that the current efforts cannot be considered as solving this task once and for all, nor can it be determined which year the danger threatening the United States is greatest. The year the United States relaxes its efforts is the year the danger is greatest. Dulles said that it is necessary to formulate a policy that can be followed for many years without relaxing efforts. Relaxing efforts means stopping the expansion of armaments; it means reducing—if not completely halting—preparations for another war. According to Mr. Dulles, the greatest danger is precisely this relaxation of efforts.

Mr. Jessup said that in order to understand the wishes of both sides, it is necessary to make a comparison in substance and spirit between the speech of Secretary of State Acheson and the speech of Soviet Foreign Minister Vyshinsky. But in order to understand the true direction of American foreign policy, to understand where the reactionary forces in the United States want to lead things, and to understand what they expect, is it not better to compare Mr. Jessup's speech with Mr. Dulles' speech? Mr. Dulles finally decided to openly admit that the United States, which leads the aggressive Atlantic bloc, is preparing not for defense but for offense, and that the establishment of strong offensive forces distributed in convenient locations around the Soviet Union is more important and necessary than defensive forces. Is it not clear that Mr. Dulles finally revealed the truth and provided evidence of the aggressive nature of American foreign policy?

Can we ignore this fact when we hear the sweet-talking speeches of American representatives at the General Assembly? Is it not meaningful what Dulles, the official representative of the State Department, revealed? Is it not worthy of our attention? After these speeches were delivered, is it not more necessary than before to be vigilant?

If our speeches need to be compared "in substance and spirit," then in making this comparison, it is absolutely necessary to add Dulles' speech in addition to the speeches of Acheson and Jessup. In the same speech, Dulles also defended the unprecedented violation of international law and various international agreements by the US Congress. The US Congress passed the Mutual Security Act of October 10, 1951, and allocated 100 million US dollars to support the Karlstein Amendment of American agents to carry out subversive and terrorist activities against the Soviet Union and the People's Democracies.

The American representative, Mr. Jessup, wanted to list some reasons to support the "Three-Power" proposal. He did this in order to prove that the governments of the United States, Britain, and France had suggested to the Soviet representative attending the United Nations General Assembly here in Paris to convene a meeting of the four foreign ministers and discuss issues that might promote the easing of international tensions. Mr. Jessup said here that we proposed that the agenda of the four foreign ministers' meeting include the following items: the current level of armaments and armed forces, and possible measures for international supervision and disarmament of armaments and armed forces jointly proposed by the four countries of the Soviet Union, the United States, Britain, and France. Jessup said: The four foreign ministers' meeting was not held, and the United States, Britain, and France therefore decided to devote themselves to preparing what he called sound and progressive disarmament proposals for submission to the General Assembly for discussion. But Mr. Jessup's memory is too poor. It was the Soviet representative, not the American representative, who suggested that the meeting of the foreign ministers' assistants in Paris include the issue of immediately starting to reduce the armed forces of the four countries (the Soviet Union, the United States, Britain, and France) on the agenda. As for the American representative, it was precisely this Mr. Jessup, who opposed including this issue on the agenda. Moreover, the American representative, with the support of the representatives of Britain and France, insisted on this position until the meeting of the foreign ministers' assistants adjourned.

Perhaps some people will say to us that in order to discuss the issue of disarmament, it is necessary to first solve the issue of the level of armaments. But the problem lies precisely here: the United States, Britain, and France insist on discussing the level of armed forces in order to use this issue to replace the issue of reducing armed forces.

At a meeting of the four foreign ministers' assistants in Paris, the French representative, Mr. Parodi, said: The French government believes that certain efforts must be made in armaments, and it is precisely for this reason that France cannot agree to the draft agenda proposing the issue of reducing the armed forces of the four countries. It was not accidental that the French representative said this.

The representatives of the United States and Britain insisted on the same position, and they resolutely rejected the Soviet proposal to include the issue of reducing armaments and armed forces (including the armaments and armed forces of the four countries of the Soviet Union, the United States, Britain, and France) on the agenda. The representatives of these three countries stubbornly insisted on including the issue of the current level of armaments on the agenda to replace this proposal.

As long as you study this formulation, you can understand how far Mr. Jessup's statement that the foreign ministers' assistants of the United States, France, and Britain had suggested discussing the issue of reducing armed forces at the Paris meeting from March to June 1951 is from the truth. No, they did not suggest this. When the British representative, Mr. Davies, defended the "Three-Power" proposal on the level of armed forces at the time, he said that it is not yet known which direction the issue of armaments should be resolved, whether it is towards the direction of reduction or, on the contrary, towards the direction of increase. Davies explained at the time that the control mentioned in the Three-Power proposal should not be understood as supervision of disarmament, but as control of the level of armaments.

On the one hand, we compare these two speeches of Mr. Davies, and on the other hand, we compare Davies' speech with the speech of Mr. Parodi, who represented France at the meeting at the time, and we will see what this proposal—the issue of the current level of armed forces—really means, and whether it is like a proposal to reduce armed forces.

I want to say it directly: it is not like a proposal to reduce armed forces, because "the level of armed forces" is not "the reduction of armed forces," and we will talk about this later. As Mr. Davies said at the time, this level can be increased or decreased. Therefore, he opposed including the issue of disarmament on the agenda.

This is a piece of history that is now being re-enacted here. This is also what Mr. Acheson said. It is two sides of the same coin: expanding armaments, which is one side they discussed in Rome; "disarmament" (in their words), which is one side they discussed in Paris. They want to reconcile these two tasks. But, gentlemen, we are all experienced political leaders, and we can understand that these two tasks cannot be reconciled. Trying to pick up weapons with one hand and "disarm" with the other hand will only lead to a dead end.

Now, Mr. Jessup said that the Three-Power proposal on so-called disarmament is a continuation of the policy pursued by the United States, Britain, and France at the Paris meeting of foreign ministers' assistants. It is indeed the case, but this means that the current Three-Power proposal, just like the position of the three powers at the Paris meeting of foreign ministers' assistants, is far from the work of disarmament and armed forces. This position means: don't talk about anything, if you want, then don't talk about anything else but disarmament, but never disarm; they say that the level of armed forces must be determined first before it can be clear whether to disarm or increase armaments.

But, according to what Mr. Jessup later said on the comparison between the Soviet Union's armed forces and armaments—a point I will talk about later—there is no talk of disarmament at all, because the delegations of the United States, Britain, and France, as well as some delegations that support them, are trying to find ways to prove that the current level of armaments is such that if anyone should reduce armed forces and armaments, it is only the Soviet Union.

Therefore, they have already stated in advance that they will not disarm at all. They are even more unwilling to reduce by one-third.

I will talk about this aspect later, and I will not say more now, because I believe that these words are enough to explain the truth of the matter and prove that the American representative's statement that the United States, Britain, and France had advocated the need to reduce armaments and armed forces at the Paris meeting is not worth refuting. Not only did they not advocate this need, but they tried their best to oppose recognizing this need and disagreed with including this issue on the agenda of the foreign ministers' meeting. The four foreign ministers' assistants were originally supposed to prepare a proper agenda for the foreign ministers' meeting.

Therefore, Mr. Jessup's remarks about the meeting of foreign ministers' assistants are seriously untrue. Mr. Jessup's arguments, like Mr. Lloyd's arguments on several other issues, such as the number of armed forces of the Soviet Union and the United States, the aggression in Korea, the proposal to ban atomic weapons, the Information Bureau, etc., are far from the truth.

The words of Mr. Jessup and Mr. Lloyd here on the above issues can only be interpreted as that they hope to divert attention from the main and important issues that need to be seriously addressed, and make us discuss issues that are not directly related to the matter, but, as we have said here, will only complicate the situation of our committee and intensify the political atmosphere.

**The Soviet Union Has Consistently Made Constructive Proposals in the UN General Assembly, But Each Time It Has Been Obstructed by the British Representative**

I don't think it is necessary to discuss all these issues, but I would like to talk a little about some of them. First, I would like to talk about Mr. Lloyd's insistence that the Soviet Union has not made a constructive proposal on the issue of assisting backward countries. In this regard, I would like to recount a fact as an example. When the United Nations General Assembly discussed the twenty-year plan for achieving peace through the United Nations at the previous session, the Soviet delegation made a completely constructive proposal, advocating that in further drafting this plan, the following should be stipulated: "Technical assistance should be given to economically backward countries, if not entirely through the United Nations, at least mainly through the United Nations. This must be based on a premise: the purpose of this assistance should be to promote the development of domestic resources in economically backward countries, promote their national industry and agriculture, consolidate their economic independence, and should not be used as a condition for the assisting country to demand political, economic, and military privileges."

It should be added that the British delegation voted against this proposal. Moreover, in 1949, the Economic and Social Council adopted a similar proposal made by the Soviet Union. In addition, the Soviet Union last year made another proposal, requesting the General Assembly to recognize that international trade must be developed on an equal and non-discriminatory basis, respecting the sovereignty of all countries, and not interfering in the internal affairs of other countries.

At that time, it was none other than the British representative Young who called our proposal "Soviet propaganda." Young also said: The British delegation saw that the Soviet proposal was not sincere in its intentions, and therefore would vote against it. The British delegation did indeed vote against this proposal.

Mr. Lloyd preferred not to mention any of this. But, this is the true situation of things. The fact is that the Soviet delegation made the constructive proposals that Mr. Lloyd talked about, while the British delegation rejected these proposals.

The above-mentioned proposal clearly illustrates our policy. Britain's attitude towards these proposals, the way British monopoly enterprises treat backward countries, which is known to everyone, and the activities of monopoly enterprises such as the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company also clearly illustrate British policy. Mr. Lloyd, this is the true situation regarding constructive proposals.

Speaking of the issue of the Soviet Union's participation or non-participation in various international organizations that Mr. Lloyd also mentioned, the first thing that must be considered is what kind of organizations these are. For example, the International Refugee Organization. The work of this organization is not to try to send refugees back to their countries, but has become an agency for recruiting and supplying plantation owners with cheap serfs, and has become an agency for collecting traitors, organizing them into mercenaries, and organizing armed groups to carry out subversive activities and sabotage in the Soviet Union and the People's Democracies. I want to say that the United States has now decided to allocate 100 million US dollars precisely to encourage this kind of criminal activity, in order to directly participate in this kind of criminal activity.

As for the International Trade Organization, we stated at the Fifth General Assembly of the United Nations that the charter of this organization is not in line with the interests of many countries, and the Soviet Union may participate in this organization if the charter is amended as necessary. But this statement has not had any impact so far. The Soviet Union is actively participating in beneficial international organizations, such as the World Meteorological Organization, the Universal Postal Union, and other organizations.

All of this seems to be enough to explain what Mr. Lloyd's statement here last time is all about.

**The Soviet Union Can Put the Numbers of Armed Forces on the Table, and Welcomes the US, Britain, and France to Do the Same**

I would also like to talk about Mr. Jessup's speech on the armed forces of the Soviet Union and the United States.

Mr. Jessup talked a lot here about the armed forces of the Soviet Union and the countries friendly to the Soviet Union. He wanted to compare the number of armed forces of the Soviet Union and the United States. He specifically said that by 1950, the armed forces of the United States did not exceed 1.5 million. But when Jessup said this, he never mentioned that the US President had already pointed out in his message to Congress in April of this year that in the previous ten months, the armed forces of the United States had more than doubled, that these armed forces would increase to 3.5 million, and that the 2 million belonging to various military reserves and the National Guard were not included, and this number did not include the armed forces of Britain, France, Italy and other countries—members of the Atlantic bloc. It seems that Turkey alone is preparing to establish an army of 400,000 men, if the Atlantic bloc can finally pull it in. If you add all these figures together, if you calculate the navies of the United States and Britain, the American military bases, and the atomic bombs that the United States has accumulated over the years under the guise of international control, then it is very clear to anyone that Jessup's arithmetic and the numbers he calculated are actually wrong. As for the number of Soviet armed forces, I have pointed out in my speech at the General Assembly that it is only half the number of armed forces of the United States, Britain, and France.

If you want to know the exact number, then please sign the agreement we proposed on reducing armaments and armed forces, banning atomic weapons, and establishing international control! We can put all the numbers—including every machine gun and every soldier—on the table, and you can see how many armed forces we have, and whether your judgment is correct or not. But it is too early to say this now, for two reasons: first, because you have a good understanding of our armed forces—this should be attributed to the work of the diplomatic representatives of these countries stationed in the Soviet Union; second, you think your understanding is in line with the facts. Therefore, this issue has been resolved.

We don't want to disappoint you.

Therefore, let's all sign the agreement here at this table, let's all sit down—we put all our numbers on the table, and you put your numbers on the table!

**The US, Britain, and France's Suggestion to Maintain Armaments at a Defensive Level is Only to Oppose Disarmament**

Faced with this fact, some people here tell us that the Soviet proposal to reduce the armed forces of the five major powers by one-third will give the Soviet Union certain benefits, and therefore they oppose this proposal. But what do they use to oppose our proposal? It can be seen from point 3 of the "Three-Power" proposal. They suggest:

1. Instruct the committee to draft some suggestions to be included in the draft treaty on the balanced reduction of all armed forces and armaments.
2. The reduction of these armed forces and armaments should be reduced to a level sufficient for defense.
3. This "disarmament" system should include regular gradual reporting and review of various types of armaments, including atomic weapons.
4. This review should be based on effective international supervision.
5. Regarding atomic weapons, they once again put forward the Baruch Plan, which nominally stipulates the establishment of international control over atomic energy, which is said to guarantee the prohibition of atomic weapons.

It should be pointed out that the key points of these suggestions are to establish a level sufficient for defense and the so-called gradual reporting and review, or, as Mr. Acheson said, phased reporting and review.

As for the "level of armed forces," that is an old tune we are familiar with. This is the level of armed forces that the representatives of the United States, Britain, and France advocated enthusiastically at the Paris meeting of foreign ministers' assistants. They used the level of armaments and armed forces to oppose the reduction of armaments and armed forces.

Here again there is the question of what standard to use to establish this level. Mr. Acheson had wanted to answer this question, but honestly, he could not come up with any serious and reasonable answer. He said: For example, a large country with a large population can have more troops than other countries. But he then went on to say: But strict restrictions must still be implemented, no matter how large the country is, no matter how strong it is, no matter how wide its territory is (according to the original text, Mr. Acheson's sentence is very strange), no matter how large its population is, no matter how many problems it has, it cannot exceed the limit.

So what do we get? On the one hand, it is suggested that a level determined by the size of the country, the population of the country, and other conditions be set as the standard, but it is also said that no matter how large the country is, we must limit this level to a certain number. This is obviously neither proportional to its size, the length of its border, its population, nor is it adapted to other political conditions, such as whether it is separated from other countries by 3,000 miles of ocean, whether its neighbors are friendly or hostile, and other conditions. Regardless of all this—that is to say, although this country is larger, and although it needs a larger army due to various factors—we still do not allow it to have a larger army, we still want to restrict it. Obviously, since there is this kind of reservation about strict restrictions, then this so-called standard is completely meaningless, and the statement in the Three-Power proposal that stipulates the level of armed forces is also completely meaningless. But, equally important is that, according to the "Three-Power" proposal, the work of stipulating the number of armed forces for defense is also the responsibility of a committee, and this committee itself has the right to decide whether the number of a certain armed force is necessary for the defense of a certain country. That is to say, in order to defend our country, the number of armed forces that we are allowed to retain should be stipulated by other people who do not plan for us and do not consider our requirements, opinions, and positions. Of course, no matter how the country concerned considers this issue, this committee only needs to tell each country how much armaments and what armed forces it is allowed to have, and what resources it can use to achieve this goal. Mr. Acheson called this a mutually agreed plan in his speech.

Can this whole plan for stipulating the level of armed forces for defense be considered truly serious, especially when considering that the work of this committee itself and all its measures must be implemented in accordance with the Acheson-Baruch Plan, which everyone is familiar with? According to the Acheson-Baruch Plan, the so-called international control organization must be and will be the American control organization, that is, an organization composed of personnel who follow the American policy in solving all foreign policy issues.

As for the gradual reporting and review system—or, in Acheson's words, the phased disclosure and review system, I only want to say that this measure copied from the Baruch Plan cannot promote the completion of the task.

Even in the American newspapers, there are sober opinions warning against using this so-called method of reducing armaments and armed forces. These newspapers demand attention to the principle of phased investigation of armaments, which is one of the greatest obstacles to the realization of atomic energy control. This is the truth; some American newspapers say that this principle has been politically unworkable from the beginning, and is now outdated. This is also true. The American newspapers said: After the American monopoly of atomic weapons was broken, after the first atomic bomb explosion in Russia, it should be clear that the Russians have obtained the necessary information. For this reason and other important reasons, the principle of phasing should be abandoned.

The "phased system" is a dead formula.

But the drafters of the Three-Power proposal still insist on their dead formula, although they intend to describe this formula as the latest method for solving the problem that we are paying attention to. This method, which is copied from the Baruch Plan, is not only outdated, but also unsuitable. Its main shortcoming lies here. This shortcoming is the fatal shortcoming of the "Three-Power" proposal.

These "stages" or "gradual reports" in the Three-Power proposal should be abolished.

**The Three-Power Proposal Does Not Stipulate the Prohibition of Atomic Weapons, Which Cannot Be Hidden from the Eyes of Hundreds of Millions of People Around the World**

Mr. Lloyd tried to prove that the so-called United Nations plan, or more correctly, the Acheson-Baruch-Lilienthal plan, stipulates the prohibition of atomic weapons.

Where did they see the prohibition of atomic weapons? Let's take a look at the "Three-Power" proposal.

This issue is mentioned three times in the proposal—once in the preamble, once in item "A" of Article 3, and once in item "D" of Article 3. The preamble only mentions that in order to ensure the prohibition of atomic weapons and the use of atomic energy only for peaceful purposes, effective international control should be implemented over atomic energy.

Therefore, this is only a statement of the goal imagined in the drafting of the plan outlined in the preamble. But pointing out the goal of prohibiting atomic weapons does not mean prohibiting atomic weapons. Therefore, the preamble is empty talk that cannot bind anyone to do anything. It only states that the goal and task is prohibition, but it itself is by no means equal to prohibition. Therefore, the actual prohibition is delayed. There is no statement that it should be actually prohibited. There is no statement in the proposal that we should now pass a decision to prohibit atomic weapons. This is the difference between this and our proposal, because we simply and clearly suggested: right here and now, at this session of the United Nations General Assembly, pass a decision to prohibit atomic weapons and establish international control, and the actual measures should be stipulated in a convention to be drafted before February 1, 1952, etc.

There is a huge difference between these two formulations of the issue.

Therefore, the preamble is only a statement of the goal that should be paid attention to when drafting the plan mentioned in the preamble, and it does not prohibit atomic weapons.

Item "A" of Article 3 instructs the Disarmament Committee to put forward some suggestions that will be included in the draft treaty that stipulates, restricts, and balances the reduction of all armed forces and armaments. In addition, this item also mentions the principles that the committee should follow, including the need to establish effective international control to ensure the prohibition of atomic weapons. Item "D" of Article 3 points out that when formulating suggestions for the above-mentioned treaty, the United Nations plan for controlling atomic energy, that is, the above-mentioned Acheson-Baruch-Lilienthal plan, should be used as the basis.

People tell us that all of this is to prohibit atomic weapons, although everyone is very clear that there is no so-called prohibition of atomic weapons here at all.

Anyone who is sincere and unbiased cannot deny that none of the above-mentioned articles put forward anything that can be considered a declaration of prohibiting atomic weapons, anything that can be considered a prohibition of atomic weapons.

The purpose, encouragement, desire, and instructions regarding the drafting of proposals to prohibit atomic weapons are one thing, and actually prohibiting atomic weapons is another. If these two are not confused, then the Three-Power proposal does not stipulate the prohibition of atomic weapons. This is an indisputable fact, and Lloyd, because he has no way to refute it at all, is trying to make things seem as if we are asking every country to declare its willingness to stop manufacturing atomic bombs and destroy the atomic bombs that have already been manufactured when talking about our proposals. He also said that the Soviet Union obviously believes that this can achieve the prohibition of atomic bombs.

But, we ask, where did Mr. Lloyd get this opinion? On what basis did he insist that the Soviet Union said something that we have never said? What is his basis for doing this? In front of him is our amendment, especially our amendment to Article 1 of the text of the Three-Power proposal—the content of this amendment is completely different from what Mr. Lloyd is insisting here is ours.

Therefore, I have to remind you to pay attention to the original text of our proposal on this issue, and I hope that you can immediately see the huge difference between that proposal and our proposal.

The original text of our proposal is this: "The General Assembly believes that the use of atomic weapons as weapons of aggression and weapons for the mass slaughter of mankind violates the conscience and honor of all nations and is incompatible with the qualifications of a member of the United Nations. Therefore, it declares the unconditional prohibition of atomic weapons and establishes strict international control to supervise the implementation of this prohibition."

This is very different from what Mr. Lloyd said here. It is not what each country declares, but what the General Assembly declares. The General Assembly did not declare that we will no longer manufacture bombs and destroy the bombs that have already been manufactured. The General Assembly declared something completely different. Everyone will not doubt that the issue may not be as simple as Mr. Lloyd likes to describe it when talking about our proposal. I would like to quote the second paragraph of this proposal: "The General Assembly instructs The Atomic Energy and Conventional Armaments Commission to draft a draft convention and submit it to the Security Council for review before February 1, 1952.     三国提案不规定禁止原子武器
    瞒不过全世界亿万人民的眼睛
    劳埃德先生试图证明所谓联合国计划，更正确一点说，艾奇逊—巴鲁区—李连塞尔计划，规定了禁止原子武器。
    他们在什么地方看到禁止原子武器呢？让我们来看一看“三国”提案。
    这个问题在提案中提到了三次——一次是在序言中，一次是在第三条“甲”项，一次是在第三条
    “丁”项。序言中仅仅提到为了保证禁止原子武器和只把原子能利用于和平用途，要对原子能实施有效的国际管制。
    因此，这只是声明在起草序言中所概述的计划时心里所想像的目标而已。但是指出像禁止原子武器这样的目标还并不等于禁止原子武器。因此，序言是不能约束任何人必须做什么事的空话。它仅仅说明目标、任务是禁止，但它本身决不等于禁止。因此，实际的禁止就被耽搁了。根本没有声明要实际禁止。提案中没有要我们现在就通过禁止原子武器的决定。这就是和我们的建议不同的地方，因为我们简单明了地建议：就在此时此地在这一届联合国大会上，通过禁止原子武器和建立国际管制的决定，并且，实际措施也应该在一九五二年二月一日以前草就的公约中加以规定等等。
    问题的这两种提法是有巨大的差别的。
    因此，序文只不过是声明在拟定序文中所提到的计划时应该留意的目标罢了，其中并没有禁止原子武器。
    第三条“甲”项指示解除军备委员会提出将列入规定、限制和均衡地裁减一切武装部队和军备的条约草案的一些建议。此外，这一项并提到了委员会应遵从的原则，其中包括需要建立有效的国际管制以保证禁止原子武器。第三条“丁”项指出，为上述条约拟订建议时，应该以联合国关于管制原子能的计划，也就是上面所提到的艾奇逊—巴鲁区—李连塞尔计划为根据。
    人家告诉我们说，这一切都是为了禁止原子武器，虽然谁都非常清楚这里根本无所谓禁止原子武器。
    凡是真诚和不抱偏见的人都不能否认，上面所提到的几条，没有一条提出什么算得上宣布禁止原子武器，算得上禁止原子武器。
    关于草拟禁止原子武器的建议的目的、鼓励、愿望和指示是一回事，实际禁止原子武器又是一回事。假如不把这两者混淆起来，那么三国提案就没有规定禁止原子武器。这是一个无可争辩的事实，劳埃德因为完全没有办法反驳，就在谈到我们的建议时，极力把事情说成似乎我们要求每一个国家都宣布愿意停止制造原子弹并销毁已经制成的原子弹。他又说，苏联显然认为这样就能实现禁止原子弹。

    我再说一遍，事实上我们建议联合国大会宣布无条件禁止原子武器，建议拟订适当的公约，建议这个公约规定一些保证联合国大会关于禁止原子武器的决定得到遵守的措施，规定一些使各国停止制造原子弹、把现有原子弹只是用于民用目的的措施，以及为监督这个公约的执行建立严格的国际管制的措施。然而他们却告诉我们说——每一个国家宣布停止制造原子弹，并销毁已经制成的原子弹，那样就可以实现禁止原子武器。这是笑话。这是胡闹。但是我们并不是在胡闹或开玩笑。我们要郑重地讨论严肃的问题，并作出郑重的决定。

    妄图让美国垄断国际管制机构
    我这里打算谈谈劳埃德对我们的问题的答复：
    （一）我们曾要求“三国”提案的提案人答复，他们是否同意由联合国大会责成原子能与常规军备委员会在确定的日期以前——我们提出了日期，就是一九五二年二月一日——拟就并提出一个公约草案，其中规定无条件禁止原子武器和建立严格的国际管制以保证这禁令的执行？
    劳埃德没有答复这个问题，他只是说，“三国”提案规定该委员会应根据联合国计划，或者更正确地说，根据艾奇逊—巴鲁区—李连塞尔计划，为关于某些问题——他这样说的——的条约草案或条约拟出建议。然而，这个计划并没有规定禁止原子武器，而且也没有规定我们上面提到的严格的国际管制。
    这样，劳埃德和他的同事们采取艾奇逊—巴鲁区—李连塞尔计划为基础，也就是在“三国”提案中拒绝在预定一九五二年二月一日以前完成的公约中规定禁止原子武器。
    劳埃德先生坚持以关于原子武器的巴鲁区计划为基础，实质上就是拒绝了我们的建议，因为他很清楚地知道，巴鲁区计划是我们不能接受的，而且并不仅止于我们，还有其他许多国家也是不能接受的；他很清楚地知道，巴鲁区计划没有规定禁止原子武器，而是把这问题拖到以后才解决；他很清楚地知道，在这个基础上我们不能取得协议；他也很清楚地知道，巴鲁区计划里所规定的所谓国际管制机构和真正的国际管制完全是两回事。因为这个缘故，我们有权利声明，劳埃德先生在这里代表发言的英美法三国代表对于我们的第二个问题作了否定的答复。
    （二）劳埃德先生回答我们的第三个问题时说，“三国”提案比苏联修正案的建议更进一步，因为苏联修正案只提到用以保证执行联合国大会关于禁止原子武器、停止其生产、把已经制成的原子弹仅用于民用目的的决定的一些措施，提到为执行公约而建立管制的措施。
    劳埃德先生又是没有回答我们的问题，而是再次提到巴鲁区计划，把这个计划说成真正规定禁止制造原子武器和建立严格的国际管制。我们已经指出巴鲁区计划没有规定其中任何一项。
    劳埃德先生的答复中还谈到把一切原子原料和一切原子生产的设备都交由所谓国际管制机构去管制。关于这一点，我们在过去就已经指出，这是绝对不能接受的，因为这样一来，一切原子原料的存货、一切生产原子能的工厂以及一切有关工业，如冶金工业、机器工业、电力工业、化学工业等等的企业，都将受所谓国际机构的管制或归它所有，从本质上来说，它的用意就在这里。这就意味着，把这一切交给国际管制机构，而按巴鲁区计划支持者的方案，这个机构实质上应该是美国垄断资本的机构，对于有原子原料和能生产原子能的国家的经济生活握有支配的全权。
    国际管制机构本应属于安理会
    美英法仍在耍老花样故意阻难
    （三）劳埃德回答我们第四个问题时说，三国同意，联合国大会应该认为任何真正要大量裁减武装部队与军备的计划必须包括建立国际管制机构。然而，他却没有直接回答我们提出的问题，只是说，这个国际管制机构是否设在安全理事会之下，那得看协议的条件而定。他解释说，假如“设在安全理事会之下”一语的意义了解为管制制度的执行完全受所谓“否决”权的左右，那么三国政府无疑是不能接受的。
    劳埃德先生的这种答复只有使人极端惊异。的确，劳埃德先生也很可能知道，早在大会第一届会议后半段，在一九四六年十二月十四日所通过的大会决议中，就决定了国际管制机构要在安全理事会之下进行工作。有没有作过这样的决定呢？作了这样决定的。记录下来没有呢？记录了的。这是不是已经记录在大会第一届会议后半段的决定中呢？是记录在会议的记录里的，而且你现在还可以读到这记录。
    所以，劳埃德先生关于这个管制机构是否应属于安全理事会这个问题的说法，证明了美、英、法三国政府不愿考虑它们过去曾投票赞成并且在上面签了字的大会决定。如果这个问题是已经解决了的——这个问题确是已经解决了的——那么为什么还要提出这个问题。这只能有一个目的：就是事先警告说，这个问题是不会得到考虑的。
    举例说，关在安全理事会的选举的君子协定现在已经变成了一个惯例。根据这个协定，由一定的地理区域推荐他们自己的候选人，拉丁美洲推荐它们自己的候选人，东欧各国推荐他们自己的候选人，而现在美洲的代表却在推荐希腊以代替白俄罗斯了。当提醒他们有这种君子协定的时候，他们硬说这是在一九四六年缔结的，只在那一届有效，现在无效了。他们凭什么这样说呢？我想他们可能还要来告诉我说：“当时决定的事情，就是说国际管制机构应属于安全理事会这一点，只是那一届有效，现在我们可以不受约束了。”我们应当说，英、美、法三国代表团现在显然在作责难这个决定的准备了。
    实质上，国际管制机构是否应属于安全理事会，本来是不成其为问题的。根据一九四六年十二月十四日大会决定的记录，它必须属于安全理事会。如果我要提出另外的问题的话，那么我愿意也在这方面提出一个问题，而且将来我们还可能加以讨论，这个问题就是：英、美、法三国政府愿意不愿意实现一九四六年十二月十四日的大会决定，或者是否在它们看来，由于这个决定只是一届有效，所以现在已没有约束力了呢？
    劳埃德先生关于“否决权”的话也一样使人费解。在没有详细讨论之前，我只愿提醒一下，早在第一届大会上，苏联代表团团长莫洛托夫曾就这个问题声明：“……安全理事会中所用的大家都知道的一致原则的问题与管制委员会本身的工作没有关系”，并且说，“因此，把事情说成好像某些拥有‘否决’权的国家能够阻挠管制与监督的实施，是绝对不正确的。管制委员会不是安全理事会，因此没有理由可以说，任何国家能够利用‘否决’权阻挠实施管制……”
    “因此，关于在管制与监督方面的‘否决权’的谈论是丝毫没有根据的。这种谈论只能被认为是希望用一个问题来代替另一个问题，企图避免直接答复已经提出的关于普遍裁减军备的问题。”
    我们现在看到，在五年之后的现在，还在玩老花样。我还要提醒一下，大会在一九四八年在巴黎开会时，我也曾不得不提到这个问题，我曾经声明：
    “……认为要把所谓‘否决权’问题应用到国际管制机构的工作上去，那是完全不正确的。‘否决’权当然是属于安全理事会的……。但是这是另一回事，这里问题的关键是：至少就苏联来说，没有人曾经建议过或者现在建议在管制机构的工作上应用
    ‘否决’权……。
    “……我们清楚地和明白地说：国际管制机构一定不能有‘否决’权，安全理事会必须有‘否决’权。因此，没有人敢硬说我们要使国际管制机构中有‘否决’权……。”
    依照苏联对于“否决权”问题的这个原则态度，在一九四八年第三届大会上，苏联投票赞成波兰代表团提出的建议。这个建议说：“在国际管制机构通过关于审查与监督的决定时，不需要所有常任理事国的代表都投赞成票。”
    这清楚地答复了国际管制机构不应用“否决权”问题。但是由于美国、英国、法国的代表团的阻挠，这个建议被否决了。显然，他们的计划不允许通过充分说明国际管制机构表决手续问题与着重指明后者不应用所谓“否决”权的提案。
    对于苏联所提无条件禁止原子
    武器的问题美英法企图闪避
    （四）至于美国、英国、法国的代表们是否同意一俟缔结公约后国际原子武器管制机构立刻调查制造与储藏原子武器的一切设备以检查禁止原子武器公约的实施情况，对于这个问题，劳埃德先生也没有答复。他没有答复这个简明的问题，而只说，调查是需要的。但是这是另外一个问题。这不是我们提出的问题，我们提出的问题是：他们是否同意一俟公约缔结之后立刻调查制造与储藏原子武器的一切工厂。这个问题没有得到答复。
    而且谁也不能忽视这样一件事实，就是劳埃德先生特别强调调查应按照以后的阶段进行。换句话说：就是对原子工厂的调查，不应在签订公约后立即进行，如果问题没有到达规定为原子武器的阶段的话。因此，从劳埃德先生的答复中就可以明白看出，美、英、法三国不同意我们所提出的一俟我们建议的公约签订便立即调查所有原子工厂的建议。
    （五）最后，必须谈一谈劳埃德先生对我们的第一个问题的回答。第一个问题是：
    “美、英、法三国是否同意大会应该宣布无条件禁止原子武器，并对这项禁令的实施建立严格的国际监督？”
    像我们所已听到的，劳埃德先生对这个问题答复说，大会通过三国提案就会是明确宣布赞成通过严格的国际监督而实行的无条件禁止原子武器。
    我们大家都已读到过三国提案，我们大家都熟知这个提案的原文，其中并没有由大会宣布无条件禁止原子武器并建立严格的国际监督以保证这项禁令的执行。因此，劳埃德说通过三国提案也就是宣布通过严格的国际管制而实行无条件禁止原子武器，这种说法，是与“三国”提案中的建议不相符的。
    很明显，这一次劳埃德先生又躲避对我们的问题作直接的答复。
    同样也很明显，对我们的问题的直接答复就是我们所建议的在这个提案中列入下述内容：“大会认为把原子武器当作侵略的武器和大批屠杀人类的武器来使用，是违反各国人民的良知和荣誉的，是与联合国会员国的资格不相容的，因此宣布无条件禁止原子武器并建立严格的国际管制以监督这一禁令的执行。
    “大会责成原子能和常规军备委员会草拟公约，在一九五二年二月一日以前提交安全理事会审议。这个公约中要规定一些措施，以确保大会关于禁止原子武器、停止原子武器的生产、把已经制成的原子武器只用于民用目的以及建立严格的国际管制以监督上述公约的执行情形的各项决定得以执行。”
    我强调我在这里说过的话，但是由于这个关系侵略和大批屠杀人民的武器的问题很是重要，我认为有必要加以复述。
    这是以大会的名义来真正禁止原子武器的清晰、确切、毫不含糊、绝对明确和坚决的建议。
    就是这个建议，提出了极为明确的无条件禁止原子武器连同它的一切后果、同时建立国际管制以保证这一禁令的实施的建议，这一点难道还不明显吗？
    这就是我们对于在这里代表三国发言的劳埃德先生的答复所采取的态度，因此，也就是我们对美、英、法三国政府的答复所采取的态度。
    苏联决继续努力消灭美英法
    三国提案中的上述严重缺点
    从上面所说的可以推断：目前这样的三国提案并没有提出必要的条件以实现摆在我们前面的任务——禁止原子武器、裁减军备和武装部队并建立严格的国际管制。从上面所说也可以明白看出苏联为了实现这个任务而建议采取什么道路。这条道路就是消灭在美、英、法三国政府提案内存在的上述严重缺点；这条道路就是对这提案加以修正，以保证我们胜利完成摆在大会面前的禁止原子武器、裁减军备和武装部队并建立严格的国际管制这一重要任务。
    苏联代表团认识到：在这个问题上所发生的情况的特点，是在于苏联方面和美、英、法方面的立场中间存在着很大的分歧。然而，我们愿意继续努力，参加建议中的小组委员会的工作。苏联代表团深信：它所提出的修正案必能帮助我们胜利地完成摆在我们面前的有利于渴望世界和平的各国人民的任务。
            （塔斯社巴黎十二月一日电）（文内小插题是本报编者加的）
