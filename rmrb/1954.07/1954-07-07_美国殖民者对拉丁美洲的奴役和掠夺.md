Title: American Colonialists' Enslavement and Plunder of Latin America
Author: Li Gao
Date: 1954-07-07
Page: 4

    American Colonialists' Enslavement and Plunder of Latin America
    Li Gao
    The event of the United States using violent means to subvert the legitimate government of Guatemala has once again exposed the imperialist face of the United States in enslaving the national sovereignty of weak countries, interfering in other countries' internal affairs, and destroying democratic freedom. This event also reflects that the colonial enslavement of Latin America by American monopoly capital has encountered increasingly strong resistance from the Latin American people.
    As is well known, the United States has always regarded Latin America as its rear base for dominating the world under the banner of the "Monroe Doctrine." After two world wars, the United States squeezed out and weakened the power of its competitors, Germany and Britain, in Latin America, and Latin America became almost entirely the private property of the United States.
    The history of American capitalism, from the very beginning, has been a history of almost continuous interference and wars of plunder on the American continent. In order to invade the countries and nations of the American continent, the United States has waged more than a hundred wars. Mexico, whose territory was much larger than that of the United States, has been reduced to a small country as a result of the US's invasion and plunder of its territory. Since the 20th century, the United States has continued to unscrupulously interfere in the internal affairs of Latin American countries, using various despicable means to strengthen the enslavement and exploitation of Latin America by American monopoly capitalists. General Butler, whose hands were stained with the blood of the Latin American people, bluntly said: "In 1914, I helped make Mexico, especially Tampico, suitable for American oil interests. I helped make Haiti and Cuba suitable places for New York's Citibank to collect profits.... From 1909 to 1920, I helped the international banking firm Brown Brothers clear Nicaragua. In 1916, I opened up the Dominican Republic for American sugar capitalists. In 1903, I helped make Honduras a suitable place for the United Fruit Company." (Foster: "Outline of American Political History")
    Now, we have seen figures like the US Ambassador to Guatemala, Peurifoy, and the Director of the US Central Intelligence Agency, Allen Dulles, being praised by American bosses, just like Butler back then. The US "New York Daily News" wrote: It has become "increasingly clear that US Ambassador Peurifoy played a huge role in the overthrow of the Arbenz government. For this, we believe that Peurifoy should receive warm applause from his American compatriots (should be read as Wall Street bosses and the Washington ruling group)." Hearst's syndicated columnist Pegler also publicly stated that it is the "obvious responsibility" of the CIA to "organize, incite, and exploit" "revolutions" like the one in Guatemala.
    In this way, American colonialists have established a colonial empire in Latin America that creates poverty, backwardness, death, and unrest. American monopoly capital carries out the most brutal exploitation and plunder in Latin American countries; American war planners are increasingly stepping up their efforts to organize military blocs, increase military bases, collect cannon fodder, and plunder strategic materials in Latin America.
    From 1941 to 1951, US investment in Latin America increased from 3.7 billion US dollars to 7 billion US dollars, an increase of nearly double. American monopoly capital controls almost all of Latin America's iron sand, bauxite, manganese, and platinum, nine-tenths of its copper, seven-tenths of its silver, two-thirds of its zinc, and more than half of its oil production. In recent years, the United States has also been trying its best to plunder atomic materials such as uranium and thorium from countries such as Brazil. American monopoly companies occupy many of the best lands in Latin American countries, monopolizing local agricultural production and the processing and export of agricultural products. The "United Fruit Company," known as the "Green Devil," owns 600,000 acres (more than 3.6 million mu) of land in Central American countries and controls local shipping, railways, radio communications, electricity, and streetcar systems. The American sugar refining companies in Cuba occupy 300,000 acres of good land and control 60% of Cuba's sugar industry. These American monopoly companies have sucked the blood of the Latin American people, and their profits are shockingly high. For example, the "United Fruit Company" extracts profits of 70 million to 100 million US dollars every year in Central American countries, which is almost equal to the total national budget revenue of the four countries of Guatemala, Honduras, Nicaragua, and Costa Rica in 1950. The profits of the two American companies, "Anaconda Copper Mining Company" and "Braden Copper Company" in Chile, are equivalent to two-thirds of the Chilean national budget. The net profit of "Braden Copper Company" in 1953 was eight times the total capital registered by the company in Chile. The profit of the US "General Motors Company" from its investment in Latin America in 1951 was four times higher than the profit rate of the company's various enterprises in the United States.
    Under the long-term plunder of the United States and other colonialists, Latin American countries have become deformed single-raw material producing countries. For example, 90% of Cuba's national economic income relies on sugar, 96% of Venezuela's exports are oil (crude oil), the main products of Central American countries are coffee and bananas, Uruguay relies on animal husbandry, Bolivia relies on tin, Chile relies on copper and nitrate, and Brazil relies on coffee, cotton, and cocoa. These countries must sell their single raw material products every year to exchange for all or most of the industrial products and food needed by the whole country. American monopoly capital groups take the opportunity to carry out manipulation, monopoly, and exploitation. American monopoly capital controls the foreign trade of Latin American countries, hinders their economic ties with regions outside the Americas, and hinders mutual exchange between Latin American countries. More than half (as much as 80% or 90%) of the import and export trade of Latin American countries is with the United States. American monopoly capital often strengthens its exploitation of Latin America by suppressing raw material prices, raising industrial product prices, and raising freight rates, and also often uses means such as increasing taxes, limiting prices, suspending purchases, and reducing imports to severely hit Latin American countries. For example, last year the United States stopped purchasing tin from Bolivia, attempting to bankrupt Bolivia. Recently, the United States' implementation of coffee price limits has caused Brazil to suffer a loss of 54 million US dollars every year.
    At the same time, American monopoly enterprises collude with a small number of large landowners in Latin America to maintain the medieval feudal land ownership system. In Latin American countries, 5% of landowners (including foreign monopoly companies) occupy 75% of all arable land, while most farmers have no land. Cruel land rent exploitation and serf-like enslavement have led to a surprising decline in agriculture in Latin America. From 1934 to 1938, the output of five major grains in Latin America was 28.7 million tons, while in 1951 it dropped to 24.5 million tons, a decrease of 15%. The vast majority of the population of Latin American countries is trapped in poverty and bankruptcy. Latin America, which is rich in natural resources, has become a "dark continent" in the world.
    The US Pentagon is stepping up its efforts to build Latin America into a war base. At the Rio de Janeiro Conference in 1947, the United States forced Latin American countries to undertake the obligation of engaging in "military cooperation" with the United States. In recent years, under the guise of "common security," it has forced Latin American countries to sign bilateral military agreements with the United States. Brazil, Colombia, Chile, Uruguay, Peru, Cuba, Ecuador, and other countries have been tied to the American war chariot through such agreements. The United States establishes military bases on their territories, and sends "military delegations," "advisers," and so-called "technicians" to control the military, political, and economic institutions of these countries, and forces them to increase military spending significantly every year to implement the US policy of arms expansion and war preparation. As long as the governments of Latin American countries slightly contradict the will of the United States, the United States will exert barbaric pressure on them, or launch a coup d'Ã©tat to overthrow them.
    Under these circumstances, American colonialists have aroused increasingly strong resistance from the people of Latin American countries. In recent years, the struggle of Latin American workers for survival rights has greatly developed. The voice of farmers demanding land reform is also getting louder and louder. An increasingly broad social stratum has joined the movement to resist the enslavement of American monopoly capital and oppose turning Latin American countries into American war bases. This powerful people's movement has forced the governments of some Latin American countries to refuse to sign bilateral military treaties with the United States, and some governments have openly opposed American monopoly capital. Guatemala is the most prominent example. Some of the achievements that the Guatemalan people have made in opposing the enslavement of American monopoly capital have had a significant impact in Latin America. As the two American writers Travis and Magill, who visited Guatemala, said: "A fresh wind is blowing across Latin America... This fresh wind of freedom and liberation brings hope to the millions of enslaved and trampled people." More and more Latin American people see that only by getting rid of the enslavement of American monopoly capital can they strive for their own survival and development. Therefore, the national liberation movement is booming. Driven by the people's movement, the Bolivian government also announced in 1952 that it would nationalize the tin mines belonging to foreigners, and promulgated the Land Reform Law in August 1953. Many Latin American countries that used to follow the United States' orders also put forward many proposals related to economic issues at the "Tenth Pan-American Conference" in March this year, uniting to resist American economic plunder. The bills on land reform in Latin American countries proposed by Guatemala and Bolivia were passed by an absolute majority. These facts show that the foundation of the American colonial empire in Latin America has begun to shake.
