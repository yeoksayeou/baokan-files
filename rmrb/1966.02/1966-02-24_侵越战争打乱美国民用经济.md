　　侵越战争打乱美国民用经济
    美国《时代》周刊在一月十四日发表一篇综合报道，反映了美国经济在逐步转上战争轨道中，一些经济部门开始呈现紧张状态的困难情况。现摘译发表如下：
    越南战争对美国经济正无可避免地、无情地开始使许多部门感受紧张。从滚珠轴承到高级合金钢一系列民用商品，部分地由于军事订货的缘故，推迟了交货的日期。从硫磺到制鞋皮革好几种商品的价格都上涨了。五角大楼已经宣布了对服装、玻璃、铜丝、电子装置的优先购买权，一年间优先购买的商品增加了一倍，达到年率三亿八千万美元。
    熟练劳工情况最为紧张，劳工统计局负责人罗思说，国防工业无法找到足够的工具和铸模制造工、电工、工程师、科学家或数学家。由于军队要从一九六六年加入劳动大军的三十五万超过二十岁的男子中抽走三十万人，罗思估计说，“有些工序将不得不重新设计，以便妇女、老年工人、年轻人和做半工的人也能够干。”
    本周全国采购商协会宣布了自朝鲜战争以来最长的供不应求的物品清单：铝、锑、铜、黄铜、铸件、硬木、轴承、纸和纸制品、棉纱、染料、纯碱、硫磺、电气设备、甲醛、瓷绝缘器、锌等等。硬铝合金部件，例如美国铝公司的无缝铝管等的交货日期从正常的一个月延长到四个月。去年秋天，匹兹堡的坩埚钢公司，由于缺少用来硬化工具和不锈钢的钼，不得不四度短期停止一些熔化工序。
    男裤的缺乏较为紧迫。上周，国防物资供应署订购了五十五万条布裤。这已经是一个月内该署第三次优先购买男裤了。为了生产总数为二百五十万条的军裤，三十四家服装厂不得不搁置民用服装生产。一些制造商预言，今年春天的男服，裤子和大衣的交货时间将被推迟。制鞋的皮革尚不缺乏，但是皮革的价格在六个月内上涨了百分之十二，这主要是由于政府对军用皮靴的订购（上周政府就订购了一百一十万双）。尽管五角大楼动用了存货以减轻价格的压力，奎宁的价格仍然从两年前的每唡四角五分上升到三元二角五分。
    军事当局依赖定期的美国航空公司班机每月运送三十七万一千六百八十四名军事人员到世界各地。军方每天运往南越的二百二十吨货物中，有一半是租借民间航空公司的喷气式飞机来运输的。
    国防部乘去年夏天海运罢工的机会，从停驶的一百零六艘挂美国旗的货船中租用了三十四艘。这支租用的船队正把南越有限的港口挤得满满的。承包商正争先恐后地建造更多的码头。这样，五角大楼就有时间重新启用第二次世界大战时期留下来的九百八十三艘货船。到现在为止，已经有六十二艘——都是又旧、又慢、又小的——重新服役了，上个月国防部长麦克纳马拉还命令再启用二十五艘。要找到足够的机师、船员来掌管所有这些船只，可能是困难的。事实上，有些船只开行时人员并不足够。

　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　Abstracts of Foreign Views

    Vietnam War Disrupts U.S. Civilian Economy
    U.S. *Time* magazine published a comprehensive report on January 14, reflecting the difficult situation in which some economic sectors are beginning to show signs of strain as the U.S. economy gradually shifts to a war footing. Excerpts and translations are published below:
    The Vietnam War is inevitably and relentlessly beginning to make many sectors of the U.S. economy feel the strain. From ball bearings to high-grade alloy steel, the delivery dates of a series of civilian commodities have been delayed, partly due to military orders. The prices of several commodities, from sulfur to shoe leather, have risen. The Pentagon has announced priority purchases for clothing, glass, copper wire, and electronic devices, doubling the amount of priority purchases in one year to an annual rate of $380 million.
    The situation for skilled labor is the most strained. Ross, the head of the Bureau of Labor Statistics, said that the defense industry cannot find enough tool and die makers, electricians, engineers, scientists, or mathematicians. Because the military will take 300,000 of the 350,000 men over the age of 20 who will join the labor force in 1966, Ross estimates that "some processes will have to be redesigned so that women, older workers, young people, and part-time workers can do the work."
    This week, the National Association of Purchasing Managers announced the longest list of items in short supply since the Korean War: aluminum, antimony, copper, brass, castings, hardwood, bearings, paper and paper products, cotton yarn, dyes, soda ash, sulfur, electrical equipment, formaldehyde, porcelain insulators, zinc, and so on. The delivery date for hard aluminum alloy parts, such as seamless aluminum tubes from the Aluminum Company of America, has been extended from the normal one month to four months. Last fall, Crucible Steel Company of Pittsburgh had to temporarily halt some smelting processes four times due to a lack of molybdenum used to harden tools and stainless steel.
    The shortage of men's pants is more urgent. Last week, the Defense Supply Agency ordered 550,000 pairs of cloth pants. This was the agency's third priority purchase of men's pants within a month. To produce a total of 2.5 million pairs of military pants, 34 clothing factories had to suspend civilian clothing production. Some manufacturers predict that the delivery time for men's clothing, pants and coats, will be delayed this spring. There is no shortage of shoe leather, but the price of leather has risen by 12 percent in six months, mainly due to government orders for military boots (the government ordered 1.1 million pairs last week). Although the Pentagon has used inventory to alleviate price pressures, the price of quinine has still risen from 45 cents per tael two years ago to 3.25 yuan.
    Military authorities rely on regular U.S. airline flights to transport 371,684 military personnel around the world each month. Half of the 220 tons of cargo that the military transports to South Vietnam every day is transported by leased jet aircraft from civilian airlines.
    Taking advantage of last summer's maritime strike, the Department of Defense chartered 34 of the 106 U.S.-flagged freighters that were out of service. This chartered fleet is crowding South Vietnam's limited ports. Contractors are scrambling to build more docks. In this way, the Pentagon has time to re-commission 983 freighters left over from World War II. So far, 62 of them—all old, slow, and small—have been put back into service, and last month Secretary of Defense McNamara ordered another 25 to be re-commissioned. Finding enough pilots and crew to manage all these ships may be difficult. In fact, some ships sail with insufficient personnel.
