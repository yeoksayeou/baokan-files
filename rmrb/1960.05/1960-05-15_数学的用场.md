How to Calculate the Area of a Slope

Whether from a theoretical perspective or in practical calculations, calculating the surface area of an irregular solid is always a relatively difficult task. The term "relatively difficult" means it is more challenging than calculating the area of a flat surface or the volume of a solid. However, geologists and mining experts have their own good methods suitable for practical use. Although these methods cannot provide an exact surface area, they can provide reasonably accurate estimates for terrains with slopes that are not too steep. Sometimes, dividing the area into sections (based on similar slopes) and then summing the results can improve precision.

Before introducing these methods, we will first explain some simple facts. These facts, combined with the method of "estimating curved surfaces with planes," lead to the formulas.

For a sloped line with length AB, the length of its projection on the horizontal plane is A'B'. The relationship between them is:

ABcosθ = A'B', where θ is the angle between the sloped line and the horizontal plane (also called the horizontal angle). The Pythagorean theorem (Shang Gao theorem) tells us AB² = A'B'² + BP² ( = A'B'²(1 + tan²θ)), where BP is the difference in elevation between points A and B. Extending this principle to area:

Assume there is a parallelogram ABCD, with sides AB and CD both parallel to the horizontal plane. The projection of ABCD on the horizontal plane is A'B'C'D'. Then the relationship between the areas is:

ABCD cosθ = A'B'C'D', where θ is the horizontal angle of the plane ABCD. Similarly, we also have (ABCD)² = (A'B'C'D')² + (CDPQ)², where CDPQ is a rectangle. Its height is the elevation difference h (the elevation difference between AB and CD), and its base length is AB (= PQ). Therefore, the area of CDPQ is equal to h·AB, that is:

ABCD = √(A'B'C'D')² + (h·AB)²

Based on these principles, we introduce methods for calculating the area of a slope. If there is a map with contour lines and an elevation difference of h, within the area to be calculated, from low to high, the contour lines are l0, l1, ..., ln-1, ln. We also use these symbols to represent the lengths of these contour lines. We first measure the area between l0 and l1 on the map, calling it B0. Then, the slope area between l0 and l1 is √(B0² + (l0h)²). We calculate this for each interval and sum them up to obtain the approximate value of the slope area.

This is basically the method of the mining expert Baumann, but with some necessary simplifications and improvements.

This method requires taking the square root multiple times during calculation, which is relatively troublesome. Geographers often use a Yurkov method, which is easier to calculate but less precise.

First, calculate the area B of the region to be measured from the map, and then find the average inclination angle θ:

tanθ = h·L / B

Here, L is the total length of the contour lines: l0 + l1 + ... + ln-1. Then, the slope area can be estimated using the following formula: B / cosθ = Bsecθ. Since sec²θ = 1 + tan²θ, then

Bsecθ = B√(1 + tan²θ) = √(B² + (h·L)²)

In theory, the areas estimated by these methods are generally smaller than the actual areas. To be more precise, only for some very special terrains can the results given by these methods infinitely approach the true slope area. Yurkov's method is only possible for shapes like the Temple of Heaven's roof, pyramids, or yurts. Baumann's method has a slightly wider range, but is only applicable to shapes like the White Pagoda (Beihai) or gourds.

It is suggested that when estimating ore deposits, if high precision is not required, one might as well use the geographer's method to save labor. Conversely, when slightly higher precision is required, we may use the mining expert's method. Detailed comparisons and error estimations are not discussed here, but it is worth mentioning that dividing the area into sections based on "similar slopes," calculating them separately, and then summing the results can increase precision.
