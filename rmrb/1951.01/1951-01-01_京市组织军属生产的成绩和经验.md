Title: Achievements and Experiences in Beijing's Organization of Military Dependents' Production
Author: Lin Hong
Date: 1951-01-01
Page: 3

　　 京市组织军属生产的成绩和经验
　　 林洪
    中国人民政协共同纲领第二十五条规定，对于革命烈士和革命军人的家属，其生活困难者应受国家和社会的优待。但在目前国家财政经济困难的情况下，各地如何利用有限的优抚粮，有效地解决贫苦烈、军、工属的生活，是优抚工作中的一个中心问题。一年来京市人民政府在解决这一问题上，获得了一些显著的成绩和经验。
    据市人民政府民政局统计，城区有烈、军、工属一万三千四百二十四户，五万零六百五十三人。其中因生活困难需要享受优待的，有六千一百九十四户，一万六千二百二十五人。市人民政府从地方粮中拨出来的优抚粮，全年约为一百七十四万余斤小米，分配给郊区的是三十二万斤。若以这余下的一百四十多万斤米，来维持一万六千多人的生活，显然是不够的。因此，京市人民政府根据城市的特点，在解决这一问题上采取了“以组织军属生产为主，结合介绍职业和实物优待”的方针。一九五○年的一年中，在各区区公所的具体领导下，基本上贯彻了这一方针，普遍进行了组织军属生产的工作。据一九五○年十二月份统计，城内九个区共有军属生产单位六十六个，其中包括制证章、制药棉、制鞋、纺毛、洗衣、缝纫、磨粉、制煤球、打草绳、装订、搬运等二十七种，参加生产的烈、军、工属约五千一百多人。截至一九五○年十一月底止，六十六个生产单位共收入工资和盈利九十二万多斤小米，占城区应发优抚粮的百分之六十多。若以每人每月的生活费按六十斤小米计算，可以解决一万四千四百多人一个月的生活。在介绍职业方面，截至一九五○年十二月份止共介绍了约一千零五十人。若连获得职业的本人在内平均解决两个半人的生活，约可解决两千六百三十多人的生活。再加上政府平日的实物优待，一年中这一万六千多人的生活，基本上获得适当解决。这是一个相当大的成绩。
    京市在组织军属生产的初期、碰到三个较大的问题：缺少资金、缺乏领导管理生产的经验和生产技术。这三个问题的适当解决，才能使军属生产开展起来，达到部分解决军属生活的预期的目的。否则生产不易搞起来，即使草率地搞起来，也可能因经营管理不善而遭到赔累，这就会影响烈、军、工属原应享受的优待。京市在解决这三个问题的过程中，获得了两个较为成功的经验。
    第一，在目前的情况下，军属生产以组织手工业性的临时加工工厂较为合宜。因为它有三个好处：第一不需要太多的生产设备和流动资金；第二生产技术较为简单，很快就能够学会，而且领导和管理也比较容易；第三受市场波动的影响较小，一旦情况发生变化，易于转业，而且在生产成品和房屋机器设备上不会受到过大的损失。再有就是这种生产的本小利大，用人多，生产的面也较广泛，只要多方进行联络，不会发愁没有工作做。在京市各区所组织领导的六十六个生产单位中，绝大多数是手工业性的加工工厂，因此投入这一些生产的资金，全年仅五十二万多斤小米，而所获工资和盈利即达九十二万余斤。如第四区所属的军属生产有纺毛厂、擦铜厂、制碱厂、洗衣组和豆腐坊五个单位，共投入资金一万七千多斤小米，截至一九五○年十一月底止，共得工资米十六万四千多斤，解决了二百五十个军属的生活问题，并有盈利四千四百斤小米。在一九五○年初，各区还未普遍掌握这一方针的时候，有些区搞较正规化的工业生产，结果不但不能解决军属的生活，反而赔累倒闭。如一区的电碾磨工厂，投资在工厂设备上的资金，就有十万四千斤小米。因当时粮食加工生产过剩，机器无法全部转动，收入甚微，致使这样大批的优待粮积压下来，不能发给军属，引起大家的不满。
    其次，在组织领导军属的生产上，应放手依靠群众和加强督促检查工作。京市的军属生产都由各区公所直接领导和掌握。每一区有好几个生产单位，以区公所现有的干部数量和管理生产的经验来看，都不可能由干部完全担负这一工作。经过一段时期的摸索后，由市领导机关号召成立军属生产管理委员会，是解决这一问题的较好办法。该委员会一般由积极的有生产经验的军属，及区工会办事处和区妇联的代表组成，由区公所民政科长任主任。因为这样作，不仅适当地解决了缺乏干部和管理生产经验的问题，而且是一种群众路线的作法，最容易为军属所欢迎，每一生产单位，由区公所指定一个或数个军属负责管理事务，区军属生产管理委员会则在区公所的帮助下，统一领导和管理所有的生产单位。不过参加生产管理委员会的军属，无论文化程度、办事能力，特别是政治认识等都不是很高的，因此区公所还必须加强对生产管理委员会的督促和指导。京市一年来军属生产工作的实际结果证明，凡是区公所对生产管理委员会比较重视，随时予以指导和帮助的，该区的军属生产就搞得比较好；若认为生产管理委员会成立了，就可以放手不管，往往发生贪污腐化或不负责任的现象，使生产受到损失。至于有一些军属生产单位，需要比较高深的生产技术，若参加生产的军属不能满足要求，可以适当地吸收一部分群众参加。京市有些军属生产单位，就是这样作的。
    组织军属生产的目的，主要是解决贫苦军属的生活问题，因此不能把它看成一般性的生产问题，而应与实物优待结合进行；否则将大部分优抚粮投入生产，一时不能获利，或获利甚少，这样就会影响贫苦军属的生活。京市在一九五○年初曾提出以百分之七十的优抚粮充作生产资金，而各区的军属生产一时又不易组织起来，结果造成大批资金积压，使贫苦军属的困难无法解决，这一偏向发现后，即及时予以纠正。这确是在城市组织军属生产中，一个值得吸取的教训。

Article 25 of the Common Program of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference stipulates that families of revolutionary martyrs and revolutionary soldiers who have difficulties in their lives should receive preferential treatment from the state and society. However, given the current financial and economic difficulties of the country, how various localities can effectively solve the livelihood of poor martyrs, soldiers, and workers' families by utilizing limited preferential grain is a central issue in preferential treatment work. Over the past year, the Beijing Municipal People's Government has achieved significant results and gained experience in solving this problem.

According to statistics from the Civil Affairs Bureau of the Municipal People's Government, there are 13,424 households of martyrs, soldiers, and workers' families in the urban area, totaling 50,653 people. Among them, 6,194 households, totaling 16,225 people, need to enjoy preferential treatment due to living difficulties. The preferential grain allocated by the Municipal People's Government from local grain amounted to over 1.74 million jin of millet for the whole year, with 320,000 jin allocated to the suburbs. If the remaining 1.4 million jin of millet is used to maintain the lives of more than 16,000 people, it is clearly not enough. Therefore, based on the characteristics of the city, the Beijing Municipal People's Government has adopted the policy of "focusing on organizing the production of military dependents, combined with job placement and material preferential treatment" to solve this problem. Throughout 1950, under the specific leadership of the district offices in each district, this policy was basically implemented, and the work of organizing the production of military dependents was widely carried out. According to statistics from December 1950, there were a total of 66 military dependents' production units in the nine districts of the city, including 27 types such as making badges, making medicinal cotton, making shoes, spinning wool, laundry, sewing, milling flour, making coal briquettes, making straw ropes, binding, and transportation. More than 5,100 martyrs, soldiers, and workers' dependents participated in production. As of the end of November 1950, the 66 production units had a total income of over 920,000 jin of millet in wages and profits, accounting for more than 60% of the preferential grain to be distributed in the urban area. If the living expenses per person per month are calculated at 60 jin of millet, the lives of more than 14,400 people can be solved for one month. In terms of job placement, a total of approximately 1,050 people were placed by the end of December 1950. If the person who obtained the job is included, with an average of solving the lives of two and a half people, the lives of approximately 2,630 people can be solved. Coupled with the government's daily material preferential treatment, the lives of these more than 16,000 people have been basically adequately resolved throughout the year. This is a considerable achievement.

In the early stage of organizing the production of military dependents in Beijing, three major problems were encountered: lack of funds, lack of experience in leading and managing production, and lack of production technology. The proper solution of these three problems is necessary for the production of military dependents to start and achieve the expected purpose of partially solving the lives of military dependents. Otherwise, production will not be easy to start, and even if it is started rashly, it may suffer losses due to poor management, which will affect the preferential treatment that martyrs, soldiers, and workers' families should originally enjoy. In the process of solving these three problems, Beijing has gained two relatively successful experiences.

First, under the current circumstances, the organization of handicraft-based temporary processing factories is more suitable for the production of military dependents. Because it has three advantages: first, it does not require too much production equipment and working capital; second, the production technology is relatively simple, can be learned quickly, and leadership and management are also relatively easy; third, it is less affected by market fluctuations, and it is easy to switch industries once the situation changes, and there will not be excessive losses in the production of finished products and house machinery and equipment. Furthermore, this type of production is small in capital but large in profit, requires many people, and has a wide range of production. As long as multi-faceted communication is carried out, there will be no worries about not having work to do. Among the 66 production units organized and led by various districts in Beijing, the vast majority are handicraft-based processing factories. Therefore, the funds invested in these productions are only more than 520,000 jin of millet for the whole year, while the wages and profits obtained reached more than 920,000 jin. For example, the military dependents' production under the jurisdiction of the Fourth District includes five units: a wool spinning factory, a copper polishing factory, an alkali manufacturing factory, a laundry group, and a tofu workshop, with a total investment of more than 17,000 jin of millet. As of the end of November 1950, a total of more than 164,000 jin of millet in wages were obtained, solving the livelihood problems of 250 military dependents, and a profit of 4,400 jin of millet was made. In the early part of 1950, when various districts had not yet universally grasped this policy, some districts engaged in more standardized industrial production, which not only failed to solve the lives of military dependents but also resulted in losses and closures. For example, the electric mill factory in the First District invested 104,000 jin of millet in factory equipment. Because there was an oversupply of grain processing production at that time, the machines could not be fully operated, and the income was minimal, resulting in such a large amount of preferential grain being stockpiled and unable to be distributed to military dependents, causing dissatisfaction among everyone.

Secondly, in organizing and leading the production of military dependents, we should boldly rely on the masses and strengthen supervision and inspection work. The production of military dependents in Beijing is directly led and controlled by the district offices of each district. Each district has several production units. Given the existing number of cadres in the district offices and their experience in managing production, it is impossible for the cadres to fully undertake this work. After a period of exploration, the call by the municipal leading organs to establish a Military Dependents' Production Management Committee is a better way to solve this problem. The committee is generally composed of active military dependents with production experience and representatives from the district trade union offices and district women's federations, with the head of the Civil Affairs Section of the district office serving as the director. Because doing so not only appropriately solves the problem of lack of cadres and management experience in production but also is a mass line approach, which is most easily welcomed by military dependents. Each production unit is assigned one or several military dependents by the district office to be responsible for management affairs, and the district Military Dependents' Production Management Committee, with the help of the district office, uniformly leads and manages all production units. However, the military dependents participating in the Production Management Committee, regardless of their educational level, handling ability, especially their political awareness, are not very high. Therefore, the district offices must also strengthen supervision and guidance of the Production Management Committee. The actual results of the military dependents' production work in Beijing over the past year have proved that the military dependents' production in a district is better if the district office attaches more importance to the Production Management Committee and provides guidance and assistance at any time; if it is thought that the Production Management Committee has been established, it can be left alone, and phenomena of corruption, embezzlement, or irresponsibility often occur, causing losses to production. As for some military dependents' production units, if higher production technology is required, and the military dependents participating in production cannot meet the requirements, a portion of the masses can be appropriately absorbed to participate. Some military dependents' production units in Beijing have done this.

The purpose of organizing the production of military dependents is mainly to solve the livelihood problems of poor military dependents. Therefore, it cannot be regarded as a general production problem but should be combined with material preferential treatment; otherwise, if most of the preferential grain is invested in production and profits cannot be made for a while, or profits are minimal, this will affect the lives of poor military dependents. In early 1950, Beijing once proposed that 70% of the preferential grain be used as production funds, but the production of military dependents in various districts was not easy to organize at one time, resulting in a large backlog of funds, making it impossible to solve the difficulties of poor military dependents. This deviation was promptly corrected after it was discovered. This is indeed a lesson worth learning in organizing the production of military dependents in cities.
