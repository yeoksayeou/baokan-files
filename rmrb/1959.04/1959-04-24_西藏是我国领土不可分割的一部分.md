    Tibet is an Inseparable Part of China's Territory
    Since the armed rebellion launched by the Tibetan upper-class reactionary clique was swiftly quelled, the imperialists and foreign reactionary forces, who had long plotted and supported this conspiracy, have been incessantly issuing slanders against the Chinese people in extreme frustration and hatred. They have also clamored "Tibet is not Chinese," "Tibet is an independent country," and so on, attempting to deceive the world public, deny China's sovereignty over Tibet, and conceal their ambition to invade Tibet and interfere in China's internal affairs under the guise of "Tibetan independence." However, lies can never erase facts, and slander can never withstand verification. The Tibetan people have long formed a close brotherly relationship with the people of all ethnic groups in China, especially the Han people, and are members of the motherland's big family. The Tibetan region has long been part of China's territory, becoming an inseparable component of Chinese territory. This is an undeniable fact.
    (I) The Tibetan region has long been an inseparable part of Chinese territory.
    Our country is a large multi-ethnic nation, a unified country jointly created by dozens of ethnic groups, including the Han, Tibetan, Mongolian, Uygur, and Zhuang, through long-term cooperation. The Tibetan region became a part of the great motherland through a long process of gestation and formation; the Tibetan ethnic group joined the big family of Chinese ethnic groups and developed fraternal relations with the Han and other ethnic groups within China, with a long history. It was precisely in the long-term friendly exchanges, solidarity, and mutual assistance with the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland, mainly the Han people, and through the test of sharing weal and woe and rescuing each other in times of difficulty, that the Tibetan people made their choice and decision.
    The friendly exchanges between the Tibetan people and the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland, primarily referring to the Han people, began very early. By the seventh century (early Tang Dynasty), the friendly exchanges between the Han and Tibetan ethnic groups had greatly developed. In addition to economic and cultural exchanges, close political relations were also established. As is well known, Emperor Taizong of Tang married Princess Wencheng, a member of the imperial family, to the Tubo King Songtsen Gampo in the fifteenth year of Zhenguan (641 AD). After Princess Wencheng married into Tibet, she brought a large number of Han craftsmen (wine-making, rice-milling, paper, ink, etc.), as well as silkworm seeds, which played a certain role in promoting the economic and cultural development of Tibet at that time. Therefore, the Tibetan people greatly revered Princess Wencheng, and there are statues of Princess Wencheng in the Jokhang Temple and the Potala Palace in Lhasa. In Tibetan opera, there is a traditional excellent program that praises Princess Wencheng.
    When Emperor Taizong of Tang died, Emperor Gaozong ascended the throne, and Tubo King Songtsen Gampo wrote to the Tang Dynasty's Chancellor Changsun Wuji: "When the Emperor first ascends the throne, if there are disloyal subjects, I am willing to lead troops to the country to punish them together." He also presented fifteen kinds of gold ornaments to recommend to Zhaoling (the tomb of Emperor Taizong of Tang). Emperor Gaozong of Tang conferred on Songtsen Gampo the title of "Consort Commandant and Prince of the Western Sea Commandery," and later promoted him to "Prince of Qin," and so on. This fully demonstrates the close relationship between the Han and Tibetan ethnic groups at that time.
    After Princess Wencheng, there was also Princess Jincheng who married into Tibet. In the fourth year of Jinglong during the reign of Emperor Zhongzong of Tang (710 AD), Princess Jincheng was married to Tubo King Tride Tsuktsen. When Princess Jincheng went to Tibet, she brought "tens of thousands of pieces of brocade paintings," "various artisans," and also "Kucha music"; later, she requested books such as "Mao Poems," "Book of Rites," "Zuo Zhuan," and "Selections of Refined Literature." In this way, Han handicrafts, production techniques, music, and academic ideas were further spread to the Tibetan region.
    In the seventeenth year of Kaiyuan (729 AD), Tride Tsuktsen submitted a memorial to Emperor Xuanzong of Tang, stating: "Your nephew is the late emperor's uncle and a close relative, and has been favored with the marriage of Princess Jincheng, thus becoming a harmonious family, and the people of the world are all happy." This is a historically significant document. The Tubo King believed that Tubo and the Tang Dynasty were "harmonious as one family."
    The "Uncle-Nephew Alliance Stele" jointly established by Tubo King Ralpacan and Emperor Muzong of Tang in the first year of Changqing in the Tang Dynasty (821 AD) is another important historical document of the friendly relations between the Han and Tibetan ethnic groups during the Tang Dynasty (called the "Changqing Alliance Stele" in Han historical materials). This "Uncle-Nephew Alliance Stele," erected more than a thousand years ago, still stands intact in front of the Jokhang Temple in the center of Lhasa.
    From the Tang Dynasty onwards, envoys from the Tibetan region to the Tang court were constantly on the road. Until the decline of Tubo, each Tsenpo reported to the Tang court upon death; when a new Tsenpo was established, he reported to the Tang court for betrothal. In addition, there were many envoys from Tubo seeking trade and contributing gifts to the Tang Dynasty.
    The Tang Dynasty was an era of great development in friendly exchanges between the Han and Tibetan ethnic groups (naturally, there were also disagreements and wars between the ruling classes of the Han and Tibetan ethnic groups at that time, but they were not the historical mainstream). It laid the foundation for the union of the Tibetan people with the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland, primarily with the Han people; for the Tibetan people to join the big family of the motherland and jointly create a unified country with the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland.
    At the end of the Tang Dynasty (mid-ninth century), internal chaos and division occurred in Tibet. A huge contradiction and struggle arose between Tubo King Langdarma and the religious upper class. Religious followers assassinated Tubo King Langdarma, causing great chaos in Tibet. In the chaos, some wanted to establish a Tsenpo, but General Lun Kongre raised troops in opposition, saying: "...Without the Tang Dynasty's investiture, how can one be called Tsenpo?" The Tubo royal family collapsed, and many royal family members and subordinates flocked to the Tang court. After that, Tibet split into countless small tribes, forming a feudal separatist situation, with mutual conquests and endless wars. The Tibetan people suffered extremely painful disasters. This chaotic situation lasted for four hundred years (mid-ninth century to early thirteenth century) because no tribe in Tibet had the strength to complete the reunification of Tibet.
    It was the power of the Yuan Dynasty that rescued the Tibetan people from the suffering of four hundred years of chaos, killing, and plunder. Yuan Emperor MÃ¶ngke sent troops into Tibet in 1253 AD, ending the chaotic state of Tibet and unifying the Tibetan region. From then on, the Tibetan region was incorporated into the territory of the Great Yuan Empire, and Tibet became a part of Chinese territory.
    From the thirteenth to the eighteenth centuries, the political and religious systems of Tibet were gradually stipulated by the central government of China. The existing Tibetan "theocracy" system began in 1275. At that time, Yuan Emperor Kublai Khan, because the Sakya (Flower Sect) Dharma King Phagpa of Tibet had contributed to the creation of the Mongolian script, conferred on him the title of "Imperial Preceptor of the Great Yuan" and entrusted the Tibetan region to the rule of the Sakya Dharma King. This was the beginning of the Tibetan "theocracy" system. Emperors of successive Yuan dynasties also dispatched a "Pacification Commissioner" to reside in Tibet (similar to the Qing Dynasty's Resident Commissioner), collecting certain taxes and tributes from the Tibetan people every year. The Yuan Dynasty also established postal stations and military stations in the Tibetan region, and conducted household registration surveys and assessments of local officials, and so on.
    Because the Yuan Dynasty ended the feudal separatist situation in various parts of Tibet and unified Tibet, it brought a stable life of recuperation to the broad masses of the Tibetan people. This was what the Tibetan people longed for after experiencing four hundred years of civil war. Therefore, despite the oppression and exploitation of the Tibetan people by the Yuan Dynasty rulers, compared to the chaotic situation of the past four hundred years, it still brought great benefits to the Tibetan people and created favorable conditions for the recovery and development of Tibetan social productive forces.
    After the collapse of the Yuan Dynasty, the rule of the Sakya Dharma King dynasty in Tibet also collapsed. The Kagyu (White Sect) Dharma King Phakmo Drupa took over. However, the subordinate relationship between the Tibetan region and the motherland did not change because of the collapse of the Great Yuan Empire and the collapse of the Sakya Dharma King's rule in Tibet. In the fifth year of Hongwu during the Ming Dynasty (1372), the second Kagyu Dharma King, Gyalwang Dakpa, sent a representative to Nanjing to meet with Emperor Taizu Zhu Yuanzhang of Ming, on the one hand to congratulate the establishment of the Great Ming Empire, and on the other hand to request the Ming emperor to approve the Kagyu Dharma King's rule over Tibet. Emperor Taizu of Ming conferred on the Kagyu sect's Dharma King the titles of "Grand Situ," "Diligent Duke," and "Initiation National Teacher," and "bestowed an imperial edict to govern the three departments and thirteen thousand households of Tibet." After this, each new Dharma King, upon ascending the throne, sent a representative to Beijing to request investiture. Since Dharma King Drakpa Gyaltsen was invested by Emperor Chengzu of Ming as "Enlightenment King" and awarded a jade seal, successive Kagyu Dharma Kings were invested as "Enlightenment King," and some were also additionally invested as "Initiation National Teacher."
    During the Ming Dynasty, the friendly exchanges between the Tibetan people and the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland were even more developed. This can be proven from the records of the History of the Ming Dynasty: For example, in the seventh year of Yongle during the reign of Emperor Chengzu of Ming (1409), Emperor Chengzu sent four imperial envoys to Lhasa to welcome Master Tsongkhapa to the interior to preach. Tsongkhapa could not go, so he sent his great disciple Jangsem Sherab Gyaltsen as a representative to Beijing. Emperor Chengzu of Ming conferred on him the title of "Great Compassionate Dharma King," and Emperor Xuanzong further conferred on him the title of "National Teacher."
    During the Wanli period of the Ming Dynasty, the eunuch Yang Ying was sent to Tibet to confer on the Karmapa of the Kagyu sect the titles of "Great Benevolent and Free Buddha of the Western Heaven," "Great Treasure Dharma King Tathagata," and "Initiation Great National Teacher." According to the statistics of the Ministry of Rites of the Ming Dynasty, in the 1550s, the number of people coming to Beijing from Tibet to offer tribute was three to four hundred each year; in the 1560s, it increased to three to four thousand each year.
    As the Ming Dynasty's rule tended towards collapse, the Kagyu Dharma King's rule in Tibet also faltered. In the sixteenth year of Chongzhen (1643 AD), the Gelug (Yellow Sect) leaders, the Fifth Dalai Lama and the Fourth Panchen Lama, borrowed the military strength of Gushi Khan, the Qinghai Mongol chieftain, and overthrew the Kagyu Dharma King's ruling position in Tibet, taking his place. From then on, the leadership group headed by the Dalai Lama ruled the Tibetan people.
    After the Qing troops entered the pass, in the ninth year of Shunzhi (1652), the Fifth Dalai Lama personally went to Beijing from Tibet to pay homage and requested investiture from the Qing Emperor. When the Fifth Dalai Lama returned to Tibet in 1653, Emperor Shunzhi of Qing conferred on the Fifth Dalai Lama the title of "All-Knowing Vajradhara, Possessor of the Doctrine, Dalai Lama." The title "Dalai Lama" was established from this time. The functions and organization of the Tibetan local government (Kashag) were also stipulated by Emperor Qianlong of Qing. He stipulated that the "Kashag" was the highest administrative organ in the Tibetan region. The "Kashag" had four "Kalons," who were the highest officials in the Tibetan local government below the Regent.
    During the Qing Dynasty, especially during the reign of Emperor Gaozong of Qing, the relationship between the Tibetan ethnic group and the ethnic groups of the motherland became closer. In the fifty-sixth year of Qianlong during the Qing Dynasty (1791), the Gurkhas invaded Tibet. The Gurkhas were an ethnic group in Nepal. The ruling class of this ethnic group not only brutally ruled the various ethnic groups of their own country but also had great ambitions to invade the Tibetan region. In 1791, the Gurkha rulers, using a minor incident on the Tibetan-Nepalese border as a pretext, drove a large number of troops to invade Tibet on a large scale, advancing all the way to Shigatse. They looted the Tashilhunpo Monastery where the Panchen Lama resided, surrounded the fortress of Shigatse Dzong (where a Han military officer was inside resisting), killed people and set fires everywhere, and robbed property and livestock. The places they reached were reduced to scorched earth. West of Shigatse, the area was completely occupied, and the Tibetan people suffered unprecedented disasters. The Tibetan local government's troops were defeated by the invaders, and the entire Tibet was in danger of being occupied. The Dalai and Panchen then sent representatives to the then central government, the Qing court, for help. The Qing government accepted the request of the Dalai and Panchen and immediately organized a force of about 20,000 people, entering Tibet from Qinghai and Xikang.
    This war progressed smoothly because it was supported by the people of all strata in Tibet. In May of the fifty-seventh year of Qianlong during the Qing Dynasty (1792), the Qing government's troops expelled all the invaders occupying Rear Tibet from the Tibetan territory. The Gurkha king "confessed his guilt and begged for peace," vowing "never to dare invade the border again in the future."
    The Qing government's dispatch of troops to Tibet this time was of profound significance. It not only expelled the foreign invaders and consolidated the southwestern border of the motherland but also made the broad masses of the Tibetan people personally experience the great support given by the motherland, thus enhancing the friendship between the Tibetan people and the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland and further consolidating the relationship between Tibet and the motherland.
    In the late Qing Dynasty and the early years of the Republic of China, due to the stepped-up efforts of imperialists to instigate Tibetan reactionary elements to carry out various criminal activities against the motherland, the relationship between the Tibetan region and the motherland suffered serious damage. However, the Tibetan region has, after all, long been an inseparable part of Chinese territory; the Tibetan people and the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland have had a long-term fraternal friendship and have formed a common destiny of sharing weal and woe. This relationship can never be changed by imperialists and a small number of Tibetan reactionary elements. Although the Tibetan people opposed the Qing government's and the Northern Warlords' policies of national oppression, they always harbored a deep love for the motherland and eagerly hoped to restore normal relations with the motherland. Although the reactionary upper-class elements in Tibet colluded with foreign countries, they were precisely afraid of this pressure and had to admit that Tibet was a part of the motherland's territory and had to establish contact with the central government of the motherland to alleviate the dissatisfaction of the broad masses of the Tibetan people and consolidate their ruling position in Tibet. In 1930 (the nineteenth year of the Republic of China), the Kuomintang government sent personnel to Tibet to make contact. In the same year, the Tibetan local government also sent a representative, Gongjue Zhongni, to Nanjing, establishing the Tibetan Office in Beijing. After the death of the Thirteenth Dalai Lama on December 17, 1933 (the twenty-second year of the Republic of China), the Tibetan local government, according to the customary practice of reporting to the central government after the death of successive Dalai Lamas, reported to the Kuomintang Nanjing government on January 1, 1934. The Kuomintang government also sent Huang Musong as a special envoy to Tibet in April 1934 to pay respects. Huang arrived in Lhasa in September 1934, posthumously conferring on the Thirteenth Dalai Lama the title of "Master Who Protects the Country and Promotes Culture, Universally Compassionate and Perfectly Enlightened," and established the Mongolian and Tibetan Affairs Commission's Office in Tibet in Lhasa.
    In the winter of 1938, the Tibetan local government found the Dalai "reincarnate child" in Dangcai, Huangzhong County, Qinghai Province, and requested the Kuomintang central government to send senior officials to Tibet to preside over the "enthronement ceremony." In March 1939, the Kuomintang government sent Wu Zhongxin, chairman of the Mongolian and Tibetan Affairs Commission, to Tibet. On February 22, 1940, the Fourteenth Dalai Lama held the "enthronement ceremony" in the Potala Palace, presided over by Wu Zhongxin.
    After the death of the Thirteenth Dalai Lama, Reting Hutuktu acted as the Dalai's representative. Because Reting had certain patriotic ideas, during his tenure, the relationship between the Tibetan region and the motherland improved somewhat. In the early days of the War of Resistance Against Japan, Reting also led the three major monasteries in chanting sutras, praying for the victory of the motherland's war of resistance.
    In the more than ten years after this, although British and American imperialism and other foreign reactionary forces intensified their divisive activities in Tibet, attempting to erase this region from China's territory, China always maintained territorial sovereignty over the Tibetan region.
    (II) China's sovereignty over Tibet is universally recognized throughout the world, and no one can deny this fact.
    Based on the above, it can be fully proven that Tibet has never been an independent country in its long history but has always been a part of China. Even imperialists cannot deny this universally recognized fact. In 1903, Lord Hamilton, the British Secretary of State for India, stated that "(Tibet) must continue to be regarded as a province of China." On June 14, 1904, the British Foreign Secretary, in an official instruction to the British Ambassador to Russia, admitted that Tibet was "a province of the Chinese Empire." Later, in the Beijing Convention signed between China and Britain in 1906 and in the Anglo-Russian Treaty signed in 1907, although Britain used the term "China's suzerainty over Tibet" in order to conceal its aggression against Tibet, it could not openly deny that Tibet was Chinese territory. Until recently, U.S. State Department spokesman Rippe had to admit on March 24 that "the United States has never considered Tibet an independent country." A British Foreign Office spokesman reiterated on March 31 that Britain recognizes China's sovereignty over Tibet.
    As for India, after its independence, it repeatedly expressed its respect for China's sovereignty over Tibet. It was precisely on the basis of this mutual respect for territorial sovereignty that China and India signed the Agreement on Trade and Intercourse between the Tibetan Region of China and India on April 29, 1954, and established the famous Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence. After the signing of this agreement, on May 15, Indian Prime Minister Nehru criticized in a speech to the People's Assembly certain remarks that attempted to erase China's sovereignty in Tibet, saying: "In the past hundreds of years, I do not know at any time whether any outside country has ever denied China's sovereignty in Tibet."
    Indeed, it is obviously impossible for anyone to directly deny China's sovereignty over Tibet. Therefore, imperialists and certain people in India want to achieve this goal in a roundabout way. According to them, "Tibetans" and "Chinese" are different, so Tibet seems to be another "country." The absurdity of this argument lies in opposing the two concepts of "Tibetans" and "Chinese"; but everyone knows that China is a multi-ethnic country, and Chinese originally include Han, Mongol, Uygur, Hui, Zhuang and dozens of different ethnic groups, including Tibetans. There are many countries in the world today that are also composed of multiple ethnic groups. So, can it be denied that they are unified countries because one ethnic group in these countries is "different" from another?
    There are also some people who acknowledge China's sovereignty over Tibet but at the same time believe that it must be premised on Tibetan "autonomy." The logical confusion of this argument is even more astonishing! Since Tibet is a part of China, then Tibet's autonomy can only be China's internal affair. How can it become a condition and limitation on China's sovereignty over Tibet? When the Indian government mentioned Tibetan autonomy in a memorandum to the Chinese government on August 26, 1950, it also acknowledged that this was "autonomy within the scope of Chinese sovereignty." Since this is the case, it has nothing to do with China's sovereignty over Tibet. Moreover, in fact, the new Chinese government has always advocated the implementation of regional autonomy for ethnic minorities, and many ethnic minorities have established autonomous regions, autonomous prefectures, and autonomous counties in the big family of the motherland. If it were not for the sabotage and obstruction of Tibetan reactionary elements, autonomy would have been implemented in Tibet long ago. It can be seen that some people are particularly enthusiastic about Tibetan "autonomy" but just want to make a big deal out of this issue, thereby interfering in China's internal affairs and denying China's sovereignty over Tibet. This is really overestimating their capabilities!
    (III) Imperialism has been carrying out frantic aggression against the Tibetan region of our country, and the Tibetan compatriots' anti-imperialist struggle is an important component of the anti-imperialist struggle of all ethnic groups in the country.
    If China's sovereignty over Tibet has ever been damaged, it is entirely the result of imperialist aggression. However, this absolutely cannot become a reason to deny China's sovereignty over Tibet.
    Starting in the nineteenth century, imperialist countries carried out frantic aggression against the people of all ethnic groups in our country in various forms, such as military, political, economic, and cultural. Tibet was naturally difficult to escape. The imperialists' aggression against Tibet began with barbaric armed attacks, attempting to occupy Tibet in one fell swoop and turn Tibet into a colony under their direct rule.
    From 1886 to 1888, British imperialism launched the first armed aggression against Tibet. The Tibetan people showed courage and tenacity in the face of a powerful enemy. They persisted in armed struggle for nearly two years but ultimately failed due to the disparity in strength at that time, coupled with the corruption and incompetence of the then central government (Qing court), which did not actively support the Tibetan people's anti-British struggle. Consequently, Zhemonxiong was forcibly occupied by British imperialism. In 1904, British imperialism launched a second armed aggression against Tibet, and the Tibetan people also carried out heroic resistance. However, based on the same reasons as before, the Tibetan people suffered great losses in the armed struggle.
    However, due to the resolute resistance of the Tibetan people, British imperialism failed to achieve its aggressive goal of occupying Tibet in two armed aggressions. British imperialism then turned to more insidious means. They bought off a small number of national scum and reactionary elements willing to recognize thieves as their fathers within the Tibetan ruling group, cultivated them as agents of British imperialism, and attempted to disintegrate Tibet from within. Even more viciously, British imperialism instigated its lackeys to use the name of opposing the Qing government's national oppression to divert the target of the Tibetan people's struggle, shifting from anti-imperialism to opposing the Qing government and opposing the people of the motherland. From that time on, a clique emerged among the reactionary elements of the Tibetan upper-class group that was fostered by British imperialism, namely the so-called "pro-British faction." The imperialists used this group of reactionary elements to carry out the conspiracy of separating Tibet from China and throwing it into the arms of imperialism. The so-called "Tibetan independence" in modern history has always been a dirty trick of imperialism to invade China, first of all to invade Tibet.
    In 1911, the Xinhai Revolution broke out. British imperialists believed that this was a great opportunity to invade Tibet, instigated the reactionary elements in the Tibetan upper-class group to launch a large-scale rebellion, drove the Qing Dynasty's Resident Commissioner out of Tibet, and carried out bloody massacres of patriotic elements among the Tibetan religious upper class and nobles (such as Danjilin Hutuktu, Tsering Kalon, and the Abbot of Drepung Monastery, all of whom were brutally murdered). At that time, the Ninth Panchen Erdeni, who was loyal to the motherland, had to leave Tibet and flee to the interior in order to avoid persecution.
    After the imperialist conspiracy of instigating rebellion in Tibet and driving away the Qing Dynasty's Resident Commissioner was realized, their second step was to exert pressure diplomatically, forcing the then Northern Warlords government to recognize Tibet's secession from China and becoming a British colony. The Simla Conference of 1913-1914, the so-called "China-Britain-Tibet Conference," was jointly convened by British imperialism colluding with Tibetan reactionary elements to threaten the Chinese government to recognize Tibet's de facto separation from the motherland. At this conspiracy meeting, British imperialist elements even proposed a servile treaty that was equivalent to requiring the Chinese government to cede Tibet, including "When the Chinese government has disputes with Tibet, the Indian government shall adjudicate"; "British people shall be allowed to trade freely in Tibet; the Chinese government shall not impose restrictions"; and "The internal affairs of Tibet shall be temporarily supervised by the Indian government; Britain shall be allowed to send representatives to reside permanently in Lhasa," etc. It is clear that a major conspiracy of British imperialism at that time was to attempt to use its colony of India to realize the British Empire's colonial rule over the Tibetan region of our country. However, this conspiracy aroused great indignation among the people of all ethnic groups in our country, especially the Tibetan people. The Chinese representative refused to sign the treaty at the Simla Conference, and the Chinese government never recognized this treaty.
    In 1918, British imperialism took advantage of the First World War and instigated Tibetan reactionary elements to send troops into Changdu, Dege, and other places. The British Minister to China came forward to "mediate" and demanded that the "China-Britain-Tibet Conference" be held again, enticing the Chinese government to sign the treaty proposed at the Simla Conference to seize Tibetan territory. This was immediately abandoned due to the firm opposition of the people of the whole country (including the Tibetan people and patriots).
    In the following ten years, British imperialists did not stop their shameless activities of cultivating their own forces in Tibet, interfering in and undermining the normal relationship between the central government of our country and the Tibetan local government. After the death of the Thirteenth Dalai Lama in 1933, Reting Hutuktu acted as the Dalai's representative. Reting represented the patriotic will of the broad masses of Tibetan monks and lay people and worked hard to further close the relationship between the Tibetan region and the motherland. However, Reting's closeness to the motherland aroused the hatred of British imperialism and the reactionary elements of the Tibetan upper class. In order to suppress the development of patriotic forces in Tibet, the imperialists and Tibetan reactionary elements used the most despicable and brutal means against this Reting Hutuktu, whom they had always respected as a "Living Buddha." First, in 1941, they used rumors and slander to force Reting to step down, and Dazha took over; then, in 1947, they fabricated false evidence of Reting's "rebellion," arrested Reting, and murdered him. Then, the reactionary elements poisoned Qi Que Cairang, the father of the Fourteenth Dalai Lama, who had close ties with Reting and was loyal to the motherland.
    The "Reting Incident" was a bloody persecution by imperialists and Tibetan reactionary elements against progressive people in Tibet who were loyal to the motherland.
    After Reting stepped down, a group of the most reactionary and craziest imperialist lackeys, headed by Dazha, Lukhangwa, Lobsang Tashi, Shakabpa, Lhalu, Shasu, and Sokhang, controlled the ruling power in Tibet within the Tibetan ruling group. Although there were also a group of more progressive and patriotic upper-class people and many middle-of-the-roaders, they could not play a role in the Tibetan local government under the control of the reactionary elements.
    Under the control of a group of the most reactionary and craziest imperialist lackeys, the Tibetan political situation took a sharp turn for the worse. They unscrupulously carried out criminal activities to betray the motherland, attempting to separate the Tibetan region from the great motherland and turn it into a colony of imperialism. In the summer of 1943 (the thirty-second year of the Republic of China), the Tibetan local government (Kashag), under the instigation of imperialism, suddenly announced the establishment of the so-called "Foreign Affairs Bureau." The British and then Nepalese representatives in Tibet immediately contacted this "Foreign Affairs Bureau." At the same time, the Tibetan local government even listed the representative of the Kuomintang government, which was the central government at that time, as a "foreign" representative office in Lhasa, along with the foreign representatives of Britain and Nepal, indicating that Tibet was another "country." Due to the firm opposition of the people of the whole country, including the Tibetan people, the then Kuomintang government ordered the resident office in Tibet to insist on not entering the "Foreign Affairs Bureau," and this conspiracy failed.
    After the failure of the above conspiracy, British imperialists and Tibetan reactionary elements played another trick. In March 1947 (the thirty-sixth year of the Republic of China), Britain convened the "Pan-Asian Conference" in New Delhi, India, inviting all Asian countries to participate, and invited Tibet as a "country" to participate in the conference. The imperialists also made secret arrangements at the conference. Among the national flags of Asian countries hung at the venue, the "Snow Mountain Lion" religious flag of Tibetan Buddhism was also hung as the "national flag" of Tibet. Even more shamelessly, on the map of Asia hung at the venue, Tibet was demarcated out of China's territory, vainly attempting to create the established fact that Tibet was an "independent country." After this news spread, it aroused great anger among the people of the whole country, and the Chinese delegation also raised serious protests. The "Pan-Asian Conference" had to make corrections.
    When British imperialism was plotting the conspiracy of "Tibetan independence," it never forgot to bring up the relationship between China and India as an excuse. British imperialist elements, the British representative in Tibet, Gould, openly said: "There should be a buffer state between two great powers. China should make Tibet a buffer state so as to avoid conflicts between China and India." In fact, this so-called "buffer state" theory is entirely the ghost talk of imperialism. The people of China and India have lived in peace for many years and do not need any "buffer" at all. To go one step further, if a "buffer" is needed, why must the territory of China be unilaterally selected? Isn't this a clear expansion and aggression?
    It is very clear that events such as the murder of Reting, the establishment of the "Foreign Affairs Bureau," and the "Pan-Asian Conference" were all despicable conspiracies by British imperialism to intensify aggression against the Tibetan region of our country.
    (IV) American and British imperialism are trying to prevent the peaceful liberation of the Tibetan region of our country and prevent the rebirth of the Tibetan people. However, the progress of history shows that all the conspiracies of imperialists and foreign expansionists will be smashed, and the Tibetan people, together with the people of all ethnic groups in the motherland, will advance from victory to victory under the wise leadership of the Chinese Communist Party and Chairman Mao.
    After the victory of the Chinese people's War of Resistance Against Japan, American imperialism replaced Japanese imperialism in China and tried to turn China into a colony of the United States. Therefore, on the one hand, it aided and instructed the Kuomintang reactionaries to launch a full-scale offensive against the liberated areas; on the other hand, when the situation of the Chinese people's War of Liberation became increasingly clearly unfavorable to the Kuomintang reactionaries, it resorted to various conspiracies and tricks to prevent the complete liberation of the Chinese people. American imperialism began its conspiracy to interfere in Tibet as early as during World War II. At this time, the United States more actively intervened in the Tibetan region, jointly plotting the so-called "Tibetan independence" conspiracy with British imperialists.
    In October 1947, under the direction of imperialism, especially American imperialism, Tibetan reactionary elements organized a "Business Delegation," headed by Tsebhon Shakabpa, ostensibly to go to the United States, Britain, and other countries to investigate "business," but in reality, to go to the United States to receive secret instructions from the US government on how to realize the above conspiracy.
    The "Tibetan Business Delegation" arrived in Nanjing in early 1948. At that time, the Kuomintang government also tried to dissuade the "Tibetan Business Delegation" from going abroad. If they had to go abroad, they should take Chinese passports. The Tibetan reactionary elements complied in public but disobeyed in private, secretly colluding with U.S. Ambassador Stuart in Nanjing. Stuart instructed them to go to the U.S. Consulate in Hong Kong, where the U.S. Consul signed the "passports" made by the Tibetan local government (Kashag) itself.
